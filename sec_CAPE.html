    Section 1.1 Numerical Integration

    Motivating Questions

    • What is CAPE and how it is used to predict the potential for severe weather?
    • How do Riemann sums, the Trapezoid Rule, and Simpson’s Rule relate to calculating CAPE?
    CAPE, or Convective Available Potential Energy, is a meteorological concept used to assess the potential for thunderstorm development and intensity. CAPE is essentially a measure of how much energy is available for rising air parcels within the atmosphere. The more energy available, the stronger the potential updraft and the more intense the thunderstorm can be. Higher CAPE values generally correspond to a greater likelihood of severe weather like hail, strong winds, and tornadoes.
    For a moment, imagine a hot air balloon. The hotter the air inside the balloon, the more it wants to rise compared to the surrounding cooler air. Similarly, in the atmosphere, if a parcel of air is warmer than its surroundings, it will be less dense and tend to rise. CAPE quantifies this potential energy for rising air. A large temperature difference between the rising air parcel and the environment means more CAPE and thus a greater potential for strong updrafts and severe thunderstorms. In this section, we will learn how to quantify CAPE by estimating the value of a definite integral.

    Subsection 1.1.1 CAPE and CIN

    The Lifting Condensation Level (LCL) is where condensation starts in a rising air parcel. If the parcel continues to rise, it cools at a slower rate (the moist adiabatic lapse rate). If forced to rise further, it reaches the Level of Free Convection (LFC), where it becomes positively buoyant and can rise freely, leading to deep convection.
    The Equilibrium Level (EL) is where a rising parcel’s temperature matches the environment’s temperature, stopping its positive buoyancy. CAPE is the area between the temperature sounding and the moist adiabat on a skew-T diagram, representing the potential kinetic energy a parcel can gain due to positive buoyancy. Higher CAPE values indicate greater atmospheric instability and potential for severe weather.
    CIN (Convective Inhibition) is the energy needed to lift a parcel to its LFC. High CIN means weak lift and less chance of thunderstorms. On a skew-T diagram, CIN is the negative area between the temperature sounding and the adiabat.
    Knowing that we can estimate CAPE using a thermodynamic chart leads us to the quantitative problem of how this is done. While several approximation techniques can be used, fundamentally we know that finding the area between two curves is a mathematical problem first encountered in integral calculus. In the next section, we study numerical approximation techniques for estimating a definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x) \ dx\text{.}\) After all, if we rotate the skew-T diagram clockwise 90 degrees, and straighten the moist adiabat / dry adiabat that a lifted air parcel follows between the surface and the EL, we obtain a diagram familiar from calculus (see Figure 1.1.3). If \(f(z)\) represents the curve in Figure 1.1.3, then
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle CAPE = \int_{LFC}^{EL} f(z) \ dz\tag{1.1} \end{equation}
    and
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle CIN = \int_{SFC}^{LFC} f(z) \ dz.\tag{1.2} \end{equation}
    In the next section, we will review the use of Riemann sums to estimate the areas representing CAPE and CIN in Figure 1.1.3.

    Subsection 1.1.2 Left, Right, and Midpoint Sums

    Suppose that \(f(x) = \frac{1}{20}(x-4)^3 + 7\) and we wish to estimate the value of \(\int_1^8 f(x) \ dx\text{.}\) Three different that estimate this value are illustrated in Figure 1.1.4.
    A Riemann sum is a sum of (possibly signed) areas of rectangles. A positive integer \(n\) determines the number of rectangles, and our choice of left endpoints, right endpoints, or midpoints determines the heights of the rectangles. The left, right, and middle Riemann sums of a function \(f\) on an interval \([a,b]\) are given by
    \begin{align*} L_n \mathstrut \amp = \mathstrut \amp \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i) \Delta x \\ R_n \mathstrut \amp = \mathstrut \amp \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i) \Delta x \\ M_n \mathstrut \amp = \mathstrut \amp \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(\overline{x_i}) \Delta x \end{align*}
    where \(x_0 = a\text{,}\) \(x_i = a+i\Delta x\text{,}\) \(x_n=b\text{,}\) and \(\displaystyle \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}\text{.}\) For the middle sum \(M_n\text{,}\) we defined \(\overline{x}_i = (x_{i-1}+x_i)/2\text{.}\)
    As seen in Figure 1.1.6, on each interval \([x_i, x_{i+1}]\text{,}\) the area of the \((i+1)^{st}\) rectangle formed is given by \(A_{i+1}=f(x_i) \cdot \Delta x\text{.}\) Adding up the areas of these \(n\) rectangles then gives the left Riemann sum \(\displaystyle L_n = \sum_{i=1}^n A_i = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f(x_i) \Delta x\) which approximates the value of \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x) \ dx\text{.}\)
    Figure 1.1.7 illustrates the right Riemann sum, \(R_n\text{,}\) and the middle Riemann sum, \(M_n\text{,}\) respectively. For the sum with right endpoints, we see that the area of the rectangle on an arbitrary interval \([x_i, x_{i+1}]\) is given by \(B_{i+1} = f(x_{i+1})\cdot \Delta x\text{,}\) and that the sum of all such areas of rectangles is given by the right Riemann sum
    \begin{equation*} R_n = \sum_{i=1}^n B_i = \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) \cdot \Delta x. \end{equation*}
    For the sum that uses midpoints, we introduce the notation \(\displaystyle \overline{x_{i+1}} = \frac{x_i + x_{i+1}}{2}\) so that \(\overline{x_{i+1}}\) is the midpoint of the interval \([x_i, x_{i+1}]\text{.}\) Then the area of the rectangle on an arbitrary interval \([x_i, x_{i+1}]\) is given by \(C_{i+1} = f(\overline{x_{i+1}})\cdot \Delta x\text{,}\) and that the sum of all such areas of rectangles is given by the middle Riemann sum
    \begin{equation*} M_n = \sum_{i=1}^n C_i = \sum_{i=1}^n f(\overline{x_{i+1}}) \cdot \Delta x. \end{equation*}

    Example 1.1.8. Computing CAPE using the Midpoint Rule.

    In \(z\) (height) coordinates, CAPE can be found by the definite integral
    \begin{equation*} CAPE = \int_{LFC}^{EL} g \cdot \left( \frac{T_p(z) - T_e(z)}{T_e(z)} \right) \ dz \end{equation*}
    where \(T_p(z)\) is the air parcel temperature (in \(K\)) at height \(z\) km, \(T_e(z)\) is the environmental temperature (in \(K\)) at height \(z\) km, \(g\) is the gravitational constant (9.8 \(m/s^2\)), and \(LFC\) and \(EL\) are the heights of the level of free convection and equilibrium level respectively.
    From a sounding, suppose we know that \(LFC = 5\) km, \(EL = 9\) km, and we know the information from Table 1.1.9
    Defining
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f(z) = g \cdot \left( \frac{T_p(z) - T_e(z)}{T_e(z)} \right)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we find CAPE by estimating \(\int_{5 \ {\rm{km}}}^{9 \ {\rm{km}}} f(z) \ dz\text{.}\) Computing a midpoint sum \(M_4\text{,}\) as illustrated in Figure 1.1.10, provides us a reasonable estimate.
    \begin{align*} \int_{5 \ {\rm{km}}}^{9 \ {\rm{km}}} g \cdot \left( \frac{T_p(z) - T_e(z)}{T_e(z)} \right) \ dz \approx \mathstrut \amp \sum_{i=1}^4 f(\overline{z_i}) \ \Delta z \\ = \mathstrut \amp \left[ f(5.5) + f(6.5) + f(7.5) + f(8.5) \right]\cdot 1000 \ {\rm{m}} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 9.8 \frac{m}{s^2} \cdot \left( \frac{10}{258} + \frac{15}{248} + \frac{10}{243} + \frac{15}{233} \right) \cdot1000 \ {\rm{m}} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 2007 \ \frac{m^2}{s^2} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2007 \ \frac{J}{kg} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.1.3 The Trapezoidal Rule

    One alternative to left, right and midpoint Riemann sums (\(L_n\text{,}\) \(R_n\text{,}\) and \(M_n\)) in computing area under a curve is called the Trapezoid Rule. Rather than using a rectangle to estimate the (signed) area bounded by \(y=f(x)\) on a small interval, we use a trapezoid. For example, in Figure 1.1.15, we estimate the area under the curve using three subintervals and the trapezoids that result from connecting the corresponding points on the curve with straight lines.
    The biggest difference between the Trapezoid Rule and a Riemann sum is that on each subinterval, the Trapezoid Rule uses two function values, rather than one, to estimate the (signed) area bounded by the curve. For instance, to compute \(D_1\text{,}\) the area of the trapezoid on \([x_0, x_1]\) shown in Figure 1.1.15, we observe that the left base has length \(f(x_0)\text{,}\) while the right base has length \(f(x_1)\text{.}\) The height of the trapezoid is \(x_1-x_0 = \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{3}\text{.}\) The area of a trapezoid is the average of the bases times the height, so we have
    \begin{equation*} D_1 = \frac{1}{2} \left( f(x_0) + f(x_0) \right) \cdot \Delta x. \end{equation*}
    Using similar computations for \(D_2\) and \(D_3\text{,}\) we find that \(T_3\text{,}\) the trapezoidal approximation to \(\int_a^b f(x) \ dx\) using \(n=3\) trapezoids is given by
    \begin{align*} T_3 = \mathstrut \amp D_1 + D_2 + D_3 \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} \left[ f(x_0) + f(x_0) \right] \cdot \Delta x + \frac{1}{2} \left[ f(x_1) + f(x_2) \right] \cdot \Delta x \\ \mathstrut \amp + \frac{1}{2} \left[ f(x_2) + f(x_3) \right] \cdot \Delta x. \end{align*}
    One way to rearrange this sum is to write it as
    \begin{align*} T_3 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} \left[ f(x_0) + f(x_1) + f(x_2) \right] \Delta x + \frac{1}{2} \left[ f(x_1) + f(x_2) + f(x_3) \right] \Delta x \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} L_3 + \frac{1}{2} R_3. \end{align*}
    That is, \(T_3\) is the average of the left Riemann sum \(L_3\) and the right Riemann sum \(R_3\text{.}\) This generalizes to \(n\) trapezoids as well. We write this observation mathematically as
    \begin{equation*} T_n = \frac{L_n + R_n}{2}. \end{equation*}
    A second way to rearrange the sum \(T_3\) is to write it as
    \begin{align*} T_3 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} \left[ f(x_0) + 2f(x_1) + 2f(x_2) + f(x_3) \right] \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} f(x_0) + f(x_1) + f(x_2) + \frac{1}{2} f(x_3). \end{align*}
    This also generalizes to \(n\) trapezoids to yield
    \begin{align*} T_n = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} f(x_0) + f(x_1) + f(x_2) + \dots + f(x_{n-1}) + \frac{1}{2}f(x_n) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} f(x_0) + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} f(x_i) + \frac{1}{2} f(x_n). \end{align*}

    Example 1.1.16. Implementing the Trapezoidal Rule.

    We use the Trapezoid Rule with \(n=4\) to estimate \(\int_{0.5}^{4.5} \sqrt{25-x^2}-1 \ dx\text{.}\)
    One method of computing \(T_4\) is to average \(L_4\) and \(R_4\) (the left and right Riemann sums). With \(\Delta x = 1\text{,}\) we see that
    \begin{align*} L_4 = \mathstrut \amp \sqrt{25-0.5^2}-1 + \sqrt{25-1.5^2}-1 \\ \mathstrut \amp + \sqrt{25-2.5^2}-1 + \sqrt{25-3.5^2}-1 \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 13.6455 \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} R_4 = \mathstrut \amp \sqrt{25-1.5^2}-1 + \sqrt{25-2.5^2}-1 \\ \mathstrut \amp + \sqrt{25-3.5^2}-1 + \sqrt{25-4.5^2}-1 \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 10.8500 \end{align*}
    making \(T_4 = \frac{L_4 + R_4}{2} \approx 12.2478\text{.}\)
    A second method of computing \(T_4\) is via the formula
    \begin{align*} T_4 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2}\left( \sqrt{25-0.5^2}-1) + \sqrt{25-1.5^2}-1 \right.\\ \mathstrut \amp \left. + \sqrt{25-2.5^2}-1 + \sqrt{25-3.5^2}-1 + \frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{25-4.5^2}-1\right) \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 12.2478 \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.1.4 Simpson’s Rule

    The Trapezoid Rule is an average of the Left and Right Riemann sums: \(T_n = \frac{L_n + R_n}{2}\) If a function is always increasing or always decreasing on the interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) one of \(L_n\) and \(R_n\) will over-estimate the true value of \(\int_a^b f(x) \ dx\text{,}\) while the other will under-estimate the integral. Thus, the errors that result from \(L_n\) and \(R_n\) will have opposite signs; so averaging \(L_n\) and \(R_n\) eliminates a considerable amount of the error present in the respective approximations. In a similar way, it makes sense to think about averaging \(M_n\) and \(T_n\) in order to generate a still more accurate approximation. Since \(M_n\) is typically about twice as accurate as \(T_n\text{,}\) we call the weighted average
    \begin{equation} S_{2n} = \frac{2M_n + T_n}{3}\tag{1.3} \end{equation}
    Simpson’s Rule. Note that we use ``\(S_{2n}\)" rather than ``\(S_n\)" since the \(n\) points the Midpoint Rule uses are different from the \(n\) points the Trapezoid Rule uses, and thus Simpson’s Rule is using \(2n\) points at which to evaluate the function.

    Example 1.1.19. Estimating CAPE using Simpson’s Rule.

    In Example 1.1.8 we estimated the value of CAPE using the Midpoint Rule and data from a sounding. We found \(M_4 \approx 2007 \ \frac{J}{kg}\text{.}\) If we expand this data set (as in Table 1.1.20) from the sounding, we can then also estimate \(T_4\text{.}\) Here,
    \begin{align*} T_4 = \mathstrut \amp \left[ \frac{1}{2} f(5) + f(6) + f(7) + f(8) + \frac{1}{2}f(9)\right] \cdot 1000 \ {\rm{m}} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 9.8 \ \frac{m}{s^2} \cdot \left( \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{5}{265.5} + \frac{5}{253} + \frac{ 12.5}{243} + \frac{10}{238} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{5}{233} \right) \cdot 1000 \ {\rm{m}} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 1307 \ \frac{J}{kg} \end{align*}
    Simpson’s Rule then gives
    \begin{equation*} S_8 = \frac{2M_4 + T_4}{3} = \frac{2\cdot 2007 + 1307}{3} \approx 1774 \ \frac{J}{kg}. \end{equation*}
