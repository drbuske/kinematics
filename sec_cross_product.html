    Section 4.3 The Cross Product

    Motivating Questions

    • What is the cross product of two vectors?
    • How does the cross product allow us to quantify the Corliolis effect on a moving object?
    Imagine sitting at the center of a spinning merry-go-round, holding a ball. If you gently toss the ball away from you and watch the trajectory of the ball, you will notice it curve rather than travel away in a straight line. In reality, the ball is traveling in a straight line (as viewed from an observer standing on the ground), but from your perspective on the merry-go-round, it appears to be deï¬‚ected by an apparent force which we call the Coriolis force.
    FigureÂ 4.3.2 illustrates our merry-go-round and the curved motion of the ball being tossed. We attach a velocity vector \(\vec{v}\) to the ball which encodes information for both the magnitude and direction of its motion. In addition, according to the right hand rule, we define a vector \(\vec{\omega}\) as one pointing out of the disk along the axis of rotation whose magnitude is the angular speed \(\omega\) of the rotating disk.
    FigureÂ 4.3.3 better illustrates the top of the rotating disk in FigureÂ 4.3.2. A mass moving with velocity vector \(\vec{v}\) is perpendicular to \(\vec{\omega}\) (which now points out of the screen). At any given time, the Coriolis force \(\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\) is perpendicular to both (a) the velocity vector \(\vec{v}\) and (b) the vector \(\vec{\omega}\text{.}\) Our goal in this section is to determine a means of calculating such a vector \(\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\) given \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{\omega}\text{.}\) That is, given two vectors \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{\omega}\text{,}\) we aim to find a third vector perpendicular to both whose magnitude increases in proportion to each. The cross product is a tool we can use to do this.

    Defining the Cross Product Geometrically.

    We have previously seen how to combine two vectors to get a number (the dot product) that tells us the degree to which two vectors are aligned. In 3-space, we now combine two vectors in another manner in which the result is a vector that also gives us geometric information about our two original vectors. To start, we review the area of a parallelogram.

    Theorem 4.3.4. Area of a Parallelogram.

    The area of a parallelogram formed by vectors \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) with an angle \(\theta\) between them is
    \begin{equation*} Area = Base \cdot Height = \| \vec{v} \| \| \vec{w} \| \sin\theta. \end{equation*}

    Definition 4.3.6. Cross Product.

    We define the cross product of vectors \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\text{,}\) denoted \(\vec{v} \times \vec{w}\text{,}\) to be
    • [Direction] a vector perpendicular to both \({\vec{v}}\) and \({\vec{w}}\) and
    • [Magnitude] having length equal to the area of the parallelogram formed by \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\text{.}\)
    The direction of \(\vec{v} \times \vec{w}\) is given by the normal vector to the plane defined by \({\vec{v}}\) and \({\vec{w}}\text{.}\) There are two perpendicular vectors to the plane (pointing out of the plane in opposite directions). We choose \(\vec{v} \times \vec{w}\) according to the right-hand rule. From this definition, you may now see why the cross product can be thought of a directed area.

    Theorem 4.3.7. Geometric Computation of Cross Product.

    If \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) are not parallel, then
    \begin{align*} \vec{v} \times \vec{w} = \mathstrut \amp \left( \begin{array}{c} {\rm{Area \ of \ parallelogram}} \\ {\rm{with \ edges }} \ \vec{v} \ {\rm{and}} \ \vec{w} \end{array} \right) \vec{n} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \left( \| \vec{v} \| \| \vec{w} \| \sin\theta\right) \vec{n}, \end{align*}
    where \(0 \leq \theta \leq 180^{\circ}\) is the angle between \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) and \(\vec{n}\) is the unit vector perpendicular to \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) in the direction given by the right-hand rule.

    Example 4.3.8. Calculating \(\vec{i} \times (\vec{i} + \vec{j})\).

    To calculate \(\vec{i} \times (\vec{i} + \vec{j})\text{,}\) we start with sketching both vectors as shown in FigureÂ 4.3.9. Vectors \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\) form a parallelogram that lives in the \(xy\)-plane. Since \(\| \vec{i} \| = 1\) and \(\| \vec{i}+\vec{j} \| =\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) and the angle between \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\) is \(45^{\circ}\text{,}\) the area of the parallelogram is
    \begin{align*} \| \vec{i} \| \cdot \| \vec{i} + \vec{j} \| \cdot \sin 45^{\circ} = \mathstrut \amp 1 \cdot \sqrt{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 1. \end{align*}
    Since the unit vector \(\vec{k}\) is a vector perpendicular to both, we have \(\vec{i} \times (\vec{i} + \vec{j}) = 1 \cdot \vec{k}\text{.}\) Note that \(-\vec{k}\) is also perpendicular to both \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\text{.}\) However, by the right-hand rule, since \(\vec{i}\) is being crossed into \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\text{,}\) the cross product will point in the positive \(\vec{k}\) direction. Note though that \((\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times \vec{i} = -\vec{k}\) by the right-hand rule.
    Six fundamental cross product calculations can be remembered with the help of FigureÂ 4.3.10. In addition, you may also want to note some cross product properties listed in TheoremÂ 4.3.11.
    These six calculations along with ExampleÂ 4.3.8 suggest a general property - if we change the order of vectors in computing a cross product, the resulting vector points in the opposite direction with the same size. TheoremÂ 4.3.11 summarizes this and two additional properties of the cross product.

    Theorem 4.3.11. Properties of the Cross Product.

    For vectors \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{w}\text{,}\) and scalar \(\lambda\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vec{w} \times \vec{v}= -(\vec{v} \times \vec{w})\)
    • \(\displaystyle (\lambda{\vec{v}}) \times {\vec{w}} = \lambda ({\vec{v}} \times {\vec{w}}) = {\vec{v}} \times (\lambda {\vec{w}})\)
    • \(\displaystyle {\vec{u}} \times (\vec{v}+{\vec{w}}) = {\vec{u}} \times {\vec{v}} + {\vec{u}} \times {\vec{w}}\)

    Example 4.3.12. \((2\vec{j}+\vec{k}) \times \vec{i}\).

    For some unit vector \(\vec{n}\) perpendicular to both \(2\vec{j}+\vec{k}\) and \(\vec{i}\) we have
    \begin{align*} (2\vec{j}+\vec{k}) \times \vec{i} = \mathstrut \amp \left( \| 2\vec{j}+\vec{k} \| \cdot \| \vec{i} \| \cdot \sin 90^{\circ} \right) \vec{n} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \sqrt{5} \cdot 1 \cdot 1 \cdot \vec{n}. \end{align*}
    Rather than find this vector \(\vec{n}\text{,}\) we can use properties to compute the cross product. Since \(\vec{j} \times \vec{i} = -\vec{k}\) and \(\vec{k} \times \vec{i} = \vec{j}\text{,}\) we see that \((2\vec{j}+\vec{k}) \times \vec{i} = -2\vec{k} +\vec{j}\text{.}\) This vector is shown in FigureÂ 4.3.13. Finally, you may wish to note that \(\vec{n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(-2\vec{k} +\vec{j})\) is the unit vector that we could have pursued above.

    Checkpoint 4.3.14. Calculating a Cross Product.

    Calculate \((\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times (\vec{i} - \vec{j})\) geometrically or by using properties of the cross product.
    Answer.
    From a geometric perspective, we notice the parallelogram formed by \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\) and \(\vec{i} - \vec{j}\) form a \(\sqrt{2} \times \sqrt{2}\) square in the \(xy\)-plane. The area of this parallelogram (square) is \(\sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{2}=2\text{.}\) So, by the right hand rule, we have \((\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times (\vec{i} - \vec{j}) = -2\vec{k}\text{.}\)
    Using properties of the cross product, we obtain
    \begin{align*} (\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times (\vec{i} - \vec{j}) = \mathstrut \amp (\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times \vec{i} - (\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times \vec{j}\\ = \mathstrut \amp (\vec{0} -\vec{k}) - (\vec{k} + \vec{0})\\ = \mathstrut \amp -2\vec{k} \end{align*}

    Defining the Cross Product Algebraically.

    There is an equivalent means to compute a cross product in which no angles, parallelograms, or diagrams are needed! But before stating an algebraic definition of cross product, we take a side trip into \(3 \times 3\) matrices in order to help us in a computation we later perform. To begin, we define a number called a determinant that can be used to ``determineâ€™â€™ key information about the matrix when thought of as an operator. Rather than dwell on the purpose of this number, we will focus only on how it is computed.
    You have likely computed the determinant of a \(2 \times 2\) matrix before:
    \begin{equation*} \det \left( \begin{array}{cc} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{array}\right) = ad-bc \end{equation*}
    But how can this be extended to larger matrices? Below we state how it is done for \(3 \times 3\) matrices.

    Definition 4.3.16. Determinant.

    The determinant of a \(3 \times 3\) matrix is given by
    \begin{align*} \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}a_1 \amp a_2 \amp a_3 \\ b_1 \amp b_2 \amp b_3 \\ c_1 \amp c_2 \amp c_3 \end{array}\right) = \mathstrut \amp a_1 \det\left(\begin{array}{cc}b_2 \amp b_3 \\ c_2 \amp c_3\end{array}\right) - a_2 \det\left(\begin{array}{cc}b_1 \amp b_3 \\ c_1 \amp c_3\end{array}\right) + a_3 \det\left(\begin{array}{cc}b_1 \amp b_2 \\ c_1 \amp c_2\end{array}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp a_1(b_2c_3 - c_2b_3) - a_2(b_1c_3 - c_1b_3) + a_3(b_1c_2 - c_1b_2) \end{align*}
    There are several other ways to compute determinants of \(3 \times 3\) matrices. Our definition is typically thought of as an expansion about the first row. One could expand about any row or column and, with the use of proper signs in each term, arrive at the same result. However, for our purposes we will always expand about the first row of elements.

    Example 4.3.17. A \(3 \times 3\) Determinant.

    \begin{align*} \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}2 \amp -3 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 4 \amp 5 \end{array}\right) = \mathstrut \amp 2 \cdot \det \left( \begin{array}{cc}0 \amp -1 \\ 4 \amp 5 \end{array} \right) - (-3) \cdot \det \left( \begin{array}{cc} 2 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 5 \end{array} \right) + 1 \cdot \det \left( \begin{array}{cc} 2 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 4 \end{array} \right)\\ = \mathstrut \amp 2 \cdot (0-(-4)) + 3 \cdot (10-(-1)) + 1 \cdot (8-0) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 11 + 1 \cdot 8 \\ = \mathstrut \amp 49. \end{align*}

    Checkpoint 4.3.18. Determinant Computations.

    Compute each determinant.
    1. \(\displaystyle \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \\ 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \end{array}\right)\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ -3 \amp -1 \amp -3 \\ 2 \amp 3 \amp 1 \end{array}\right)\)
    Answer.
    1. \begin{align*} \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \\ 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \end{array}\right) = \mathstrut \amp \left\{\begin{array}{l} 1(5 \cdot 9 - 8 \cdot 6) \\ - 2(4 \cdot 9 - 7 \cdot 6) \\ + 3(4 \cdot 8 - 7 \cdot 5) \end{array}\right. \\ = \mathstrut \amp -3 +12-9 \\ = \mathstrut \amp 0 \end{align*}
    2. \begin{align*} \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ -3 \amp -1 \amp -3 \\ 2 \amp 3 \amp 1 \end{array}\right) = \mathstrut \amp \left\{ \begin{array}{l} 1(-1 \cdot 1 - 3 \cdot (-3)) \\ - 3(-3 \cdot 1 - 2 \cdot (-3)) \\ + 2(-3 \cdot 3 - 2 \cdot (-1)) \end{array}\right.\\ = \mathstrut \amp 8 -9-14 \\ = \mathstrut \amp -15 \end{align*}

    Theorem 4.3.19. Algebraic Calculation of Cross Product.

    If \(\vec{v} = v_1\vec{i} + v_2\vec{j} + v_3\vec{k}\) and \(\vec{w} = w_1\vec{i} + w_2\vec{j} + w_3\vec{k}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{align*} {\vec{v}} \times {\vec{w}} = \mathstrut \amp (v_2w_3 - v_3w_2)\vec{i} - (v_1w_3 - v_3w_1)\vec{j} + (v_1w_2 - v_2w_1)\vec{k} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ v_1 \amp v_2 \amp v_3 \\ w_1 \amp w_2 \amp w_3 \end{array} \right) \end{align*}
    It should be noted that in the algebraic calculation of a cross product, the determinant contains vector entries \(\vec{i}\text{,}\) \(\vec{j}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{k}\text{.}\) However, the calculation of the determinant still utilizes the same process as if all entries were numbers.

    Example 4.3.20. Algebraic and Geometric Computations Produce The Same Cross Product.

    Compare the algebraic calculation below to the geometric calculation applied in ExampleÂ 4.3.12.
    \begin{align*} ( 2\vec{j} +\vec{k}) \times \vec{i} = \mathstrut \amp \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}\vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{array}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 0\vec{i} - (-1)\vec{j}+(-2)\vec{k} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{j} - 2\vec{k}. \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.21. Algebraic and Geometric Computations Produce The Same Cross Product - Part II.

    Compare the algebraic calculation below to the geometric calculation applied in ExampleÂ 4.3.8.
    \begin{align*} \vec{i} \times ( \vec{j} +\vec{k}) = \mathstrut \amp \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}\vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \end{array}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 0\vec{i} - 0\vec{j}+1\vec{k} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k}. \end{align*}

    Checkpoint 4.3.22. Two Ways to a Cross Product.

    Calculate \((\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times (\vec{i} - \vec{j})\) from CheckpointÂ 4.3.14 algebraically. Compare results.
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} (\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times (\vec{i} - \vec{j}) = \mathstrut \amp \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}\vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 0 \end{array}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 0\vec{i} - 0\vec{j}+(-1-1)\vec{k} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2\vec{k}. \end{align*}
    Naturally, the result is the same.

    Checkpoint 4.3.23. Use a Determinant to Compute a Cross Product.

    Compute \(( 2\vec{i} -\vec{j} - 2\vec{k}) \times (\vec{j} + 4\vec{k})\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} ( 2\vec{i} -\vec{j} - 2\vec{k}) \times (\vec{j} + 4\vec{k}) = \mathstrut \amp \det\left(\begin{array}{ccc}\vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp -2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 4 \end{array}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2\vec{i} - 8\vec{j}+2\vec{k} \end{align*}

    Coriolis Force.

    Earthâ€™s rotation makes atmospheric circulation more complex than otherwise would be the case. The paths of the winds on a rotating Earth are deflected by the Coriolis force. The Coriolis effect is a result of the fact that different latitudes on Earth rotate at different speeds. In 24 hours a point on the equator must complete a rotation distance equal to the circumference of the Earth, while a point right on the poles covers no distance in that time; it just turns in a circle. So the speed of rotation at the equator is about 1600 km/hr, while at the poles the speed is 0 km/hr. Latitudes in between rotate at intermediate speeds; approximately 1400 km/hr at 30\(^{\circ}\) and 800 km/hr at 60\(^{\circ}\text{.}\) As objects move over the surface of the Earth they encounter regions of varying speed, which causes their path to be deflected.
    Imagine now a cannon positioned at the equator and facing north. Even though the cannon appears stationary to someone on Earth, it is in fact moving east at about 1600 km/hr due to Earthâ€™s rotation. When the cannon fires, the projectile travels north towards its target; but it also continues to move to the east at 1600 km/hr, the speed it had while it was still in the cannon. As the shell moves over higher latitudes, its momentum carries it eastward faster than the speed at which the ground beneath it is rotating. For example, by 30\(^\circ\) latitude the shell is moving east at 1600 km/hr while the ground is moving east at only 1400 km/hr. Therefore, the shell gets â€œaheadâ€ of its target, and will land to the east of its intended destination. From the point of view of the cannon, the path of the projectile appears to have been deflected to the right (red arrow, FigureÂ 4.3.24). Similarly, a cannon located at 60\(^{\circ}\) and facing the equator will be moving east at 800 km/hr. When its shell is fired towards the equator, the shell will be moving east at 800 km/hr, but as it approaches the equator it will be moving over land that is traveling east faster than the projectile. So the projectile gets â€œbehindâ€ its target, and will land to the west of its destination. But from the point of view of the cannon facing the equator, the path of the shell still appears to have been deflected to the right (green arrow, FigureÂ 4.3.24). Therefore, in the Northern Hemisphere, the apparent Coriolis deflection will always be to the right. In the Southern Hemisphere, deflection is to the left.
    Movements of air in the atmosphere and water in the ocean are notable examples of this behavior: rather than flowing directly from areas of high pressure to low pressure, as they would on a non-rotating planet, winds and currents tend to flow to the right of this direction north of the equator (see FigureÂ 4.3.25) and left of this direction south of the equator. This effect is responsible for the rotation of large cyclones and tornados.
    The magnitude and direction of the Coriolis acceleration is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}} = -2 \vec{\Omega} \times \vec{v} \end{equation*}
    where \(\vec{v}\) is the velocity vector of the mass and \(\vec{\Omega}\) (i.e. the rotation vector) is Earthâ€™s angular velocity vector. Since the Earth rotates to the east, \(\vec{\Omega}\) is along the Earthâ€™s axis, northward out of the north pole and
    \begin{equation*} \| \vec{\Omega} \| = \omega = 7.2921 \times 10^{-5} \ {\rm{rad/s}} \end{equation*}
    is the rotation rate of the Earth.
    The Coriolis force on an object of mass \(m\) moving with velocity \(\vec{v}\) is thus
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}} = m\vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}} = -2 m\vec{\Omega} \times \vec{v}. \end{equation*}
    Consider now a location with latitude \(\varphi\) on a sphere (such as the Earth) that is rotating around \(\vec{\Omega}\) as in FigureÂ 4.3.26. Set up a new, local coordinate system with the location at the origin and with \(x\)-axis horizontally due east (\(\vec{i}\)), the \(y\)-axis horizontally due north (\(\vec{j}\)) and the \(z\)-axis vertically upwards (\(\vec{k}\)).
    • Denote the velocity of movement \(\vec{v}\) in this local coordinate system as
      \begin{equation*} v_x\vec{i} + v_y \vec{j} + v_z \vec{k}. \end{equation*}
    • The rotation vector \(\vec{\Omega}\) in this local coordinate system is
      \begin{equation*} 0\vec{i} + \omega \cos \varphi \vec{j} + \omega \sin \varphi \vec{k}. \end{equation*}
    • Coriolis acceleration \(\vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}}\) expressed in this coordinate system is then
      \begin{align*} -2\vec{\Omega} \times \vec{v} = \mathstrut \amp -2 \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 0 \amp \omega \cos \varphi \amp \omega \sin \varphi \\ v_x \amp v_y \amp v_z \end{array} \right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2 \left[ (\omega v_z \cos \varphi - \omega v_y \sin \varphi)\vec{i} + \omega v_x \sin \varphi \vec{j} - \omega v_x \cos \varphi \vec{k} \right] \end{align*}
    If \(v_z = 0\) (i.e. there is no vertical motion) and we the ignore vertical (i.e. \(\vec{k}\)) component of Coriolis acceleration (which is reasonable since gravity is usually far larger in magnitude), we have
    \begin{align*} \vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}} = \mathstrut \amp -2 \left( - \omega v_y \sin \varphi\vec{i} + \omega v_x \sin \varphi \vec{j} \right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2 \omega \sin \varphi \left( v_y \vec{i} - v_x \vec{j} \right) \end{align*}
    We define the factor \(f = 2\omega \sin \varphi\) as the Coriolis parameter. In this situation, note the following:
    • If \(v_x > 0\) and \(v_y =0\) (motion is due east), then \(\vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}}\) is south.
    • If \(v_y > 0\) and \(v_x =0\) (motion is due north), then \(\vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}}\) is east.

    Checkpoint 4.3.27. Coriolis Force on a Golf Ball.

    We are on the surface of the Earth at a golf course in Saint Cloud, Minnesota (latitude of \(\varphi = 45.6^{\circ}\)). We hit a golf ball due south with speed \(v\text{.}\)
    1. Explain why the magnitude of the Coriolis force \(\|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\|\) on the golf ball has value
      \begin{equation*} 2mv\Omega \sin\varphi. \end{equation*}
    2. The Coriolis effect is very slight. To see this, calculate the magnitude of the force on the golf ball \(\vec{F}_g\) due to gravity and compare it (as a percentage) to the magnitude of the Coriolis force \(\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\text{.}\) Assume the mass of the golf ball is \(m=45\) g and travels at a typical ball speed of \(v=\) 50 m/s. [Hint: You are calculating \(\|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\|/\|\vec{F}_g\|\text{.}\)]
    Answer.
    1. Since the angle between \(\vec{\Omega}\) and \(\vec{v}\) is \(\varphi\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\| = \mathstrut \amp \|-2 m\vec{\Omega} \times m\vec{v}\| \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2m \cdot \Omega \cdot \| \vec{v} \| \cdot \sin\varphi \end{align*}
    2. We calculate \(\|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\|/\|\vec{F}_g\|\text{.}\)
      \begin{align*} \|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\| = \mathstrut \amp m \| \vec{a}_{\rm{Cor}} \| \\ = \mathstrut \amp m \cdot 2\Omega \sin\varphi (v_y\vec{i} - v_x \vec{j})\\ = \mathstrut \amp (0.045 \ {\rm{kg}}) 2(7.2921 \times 10^{-5} \ {\rm{rad/s}})\sin(45.6^{\circ}) \left[ (50 \ {\rm{m/s}})\vec{i} - 0\vec{j}\right]\\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 2.34 \times 10^{-4} \ {\rm{Newtons}} \end{align*}
      And,
      \begin{align*} \|\vec{F}_g\| = \mathstrut \amp (0.045 \ {\rm{kg}})(9.81 \ \frac{\rm{m}}{\rm{s}^2}\\ = \mathstrut \amp 0.441 \ {\rm{Newtons}} \end{align*}
      This gives \(\|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\|/\|\vec{F}_g\| \approx 5.32 \times 10^{-4}\text{.}\) That is, the Coriolis force on the golf ball is about 0.05% that of the gravitational force on the golf ball.

    Geostrophic Wind.

    An air parcel initially at rest will move from high pressure to low pressure because of the pressure gradient force (PGF). However, as that air parcel begins to move, it is deflected by the Coriolis force to the right in the northern hemisphere (to the left in the southern hemisphere). See FigureÂ 4.3.28. As the wind gains speed, the deflection increases until the Coriolis force equals the pressure gradient force. At this point, the wind will be blowing parallel to the isobars. When this happens, the wind is referred to as geostrophic.
    Winds in nature are rarely exactly geostrophic, but to a good approximation, the winds in the upper troposphere can be close. This is because winds are only considered truly geostrophic when the isobars are straight and there are no other forces acting on it -- and these conditions just arenâ€™t found too often in nature.
