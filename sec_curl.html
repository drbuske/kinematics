    Section 6.3 Curl

    Motivating Questions

    • How can we understand the concept of curl both geometrically and algebraically?
    • What is meant when we say we are only concerned with the vertical component of vorticity? How do we compute that?
    As with many concepts, the best way to understand the notion of curl is geometrically. We give a geometric definition first to help in understanding the concept and purpose. Later, we give an equivalent algebraic definition that will make computation a straightforward task.

    Definition 6.3.1. Geometric Definition of Curl.

    The curl of a smooth vector field \(\vec{F}\text{,}\) written \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}\text{,}\) is the vector field with the following properties:
    • The direction of \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(x,y,z)\) is the direction of \(\vec{n}\) for which circulation density \(circ_{\vec{n}} \vec{F}(x,y,z)\) is the greatest.
    • The magnitude of \({\rm{curl}}\ \vec{F}(x,y,z)\) is the circulation density of \(\vec{F}\) around that direction.

    Example 6.3.2. Zero or Nonzero Curl.

    Measuring how quickly a very tiny paddle wheel will spin within a vector field is not easy nor quantitative. Is there an algebraic way to determine \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(x,y,z)\text{?}\) Luckily, the answer is yes.

    Definition 6.3.3. Algebraic (Coordinate) Definition of Curl.

    If \(\vec{F} = F_1\vec{i} + F_2\vec{j} + F_3\vec{k}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{align*} {\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F} = \mathstrut \amp \left( \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial z} \right) \vec{i} + \left( \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x} \right) \vec{j} + \left( \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y} \right) \vec{k} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ F_1 \amp F_2 \amp F_3 \end{array} \right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \nabla \times \vec{F} \end{align*}
    As we will see, we are often most interested only in the vertical component of the curl of a vector field \(\vec{F}\text{.}\) The algebraic definition above allows us to gauge this through the estimation of two partials: \(\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y} \text{.}\)

    Example 6.3.4. Connecting Curl Definitions.

    Consider the vector field \(\vec{F}=x\vec{j}\text{.}\) A cross section of \(\vec{F}\) that lies in the plane \(z=0\) is given in Figure 6.3.5. We investigate \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(-1,1,0)\text{.}\)
    If we place a small square \(C\) centered at \((-1,1,0)\) as shown in Figure 6.3.5 in the vector field \(\vec{F}=x\vec{j}\text{,}\) we can imagine it as a small paddle wheel (see Figure 6.2.14) that will turn based on the flow. In this case, the paddle wheel will turn counter-clockwise. In fact, it is when the handle of the paddle wheel points in the direction of \(+\vec{k}\) that the it spins fastest in the counter-clockwise direction (i.e. when the circulation is greatest). So geometrically, we see that \({\rm{curl}}\ \vec{F}(-1,1,0) = \alpha\vec{k}\) for some number \(\alpha>0\text{.}\)
    Using Definition 6.3.3, we can compute \({\rm{curl}}\ \vec{F}(-1,1,0)\) directly. For \(\vec{F}=x\vec{j}\text{,}\) we note that
    \begin{align*} {\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F} = \mathstrut \amp \nabla \times \vec{F} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ 0 \amp x \amp 0 \end{array} \right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 1\vec{k} \end{align*}
    which aligns with what we expected using the geometric definition of curl. Note too that, rather remarkably, \({\rm{curl}}\ \vec{F}\) is the same vector at every point in this field!

    Checkpoint 6.3.6.

    A cross section of a vector field \(\vec{F}\) that lies in the plane \(z=0\) is shown in Figure 6.3.7.
    1. Only using Figure 6.3.7, and not the algebraic definition, determine whether \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,1,0)\) points in the \(\vec{k}\) or the \(-\vec{k}\) direction. How about \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,-1,0)\text{?}\)
    2. If \(\vec{F}(x,y,z) = (4-y^2)\vec{i}\text{,}\) compute \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,1,0)\) and \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,-1,0)\) algebraically. Do your answers match those found in (a)?
    Answer.
    1. Based on the figure, a small paddle wheel placed at \((0,1,0)\) would spin counter-clockwise the fastest if the handle is placed in the \(\vec{k}\) direction. So \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,1,0)\) points in the \(\vec{k}\) direction. Similarly, a small paddle wheel placed at \((0,-1,0)\) would spin counter-clockwise the fastest if the handle is placed in the \(-\vec{k}\) direction. So \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,-1,0)\) points in the \(-\vec{k}\) direction.
    2. For \(\vec{F}=(4-y^2)\vec{i}\text{,}\) we note that
      \begin{align*} {\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F} = \mathstrut \amp \nabla \times \vec{F} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ 4-y^2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{array} \right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2y\vec{k} \end{align*}
      so that \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,1,0) = 2\vec{k}\) and \({\rm{curl}} \ \vec{F}(0,-1,0) = -2\vec{k}\text{.}\) This aligns with what was found in (a).

    Vorticity.

    Vorticity in fluids is similar to angular momentum in solids. Just like curl, it is easiest to visualize vorticity by thinking of a small paddle wheel immersed in the fluid. If the fluid flow turns the paddle wheel, then it has vorticity. Vorticity is a vector, and points out of the plane in which the fluid turns. The sign of the vorticity is given by the right-hand rule. If you curl the fingers on your right hand in the direction of the turning paddle wheel and your thumb points upward, then the vorticity is positive. If your thumb points downward, the vorticity is negative. Does this sound familiar? It should! If not, review the concept of curl in the last section.

    Definition 6.3.8. Vorticity.

    Vorticity is a vector measure of local rotation in a fluid flow, defined as the curl of the velocity vector,
    \begin{equation*} \nu = \nabla \times \vec{v}, \end{equation*}
    where \(\nu\) is the vorticity, \(\vec{v}\) the velocity, and \(\nabla\) the gradient operator.
    Vorticity, therefore, has units of inverse time, for instance, \(({\rm{sec}})^{-1}\text{.}\) Fluids (and all objects) have vorticity simply because of Earth’s rotation. This is called planetary vorticity. We do not normally appreciate this component of vorticity since it is only important if a motion lasts for a significant portion of a day, and most important if it lasts for many days, months, or years. Since geostrophic motion is essentially steady compared with the rotation time of Earth, planetary vorticity is very important for nearly geostrophic flows. The vector of planetary vorticity \(f\) points upward, parallel to the rotation axis of Earth. Its magnitude is twice the angular rotation rate \(\Omega\) of Earth.

    Checkpoint 6.3.9. Planetary Vorticity.

    The magnitude of planetary vorticity \(\| f \|\) is twice the angular rotation rate \((\Omega = 2\pi/{\rm{day}})\) of Earth. Compute \(\| f \|\) in units of \(({\rm{sec}})^{-1}\text{.}\) Then, sketch the Earth (with axis) and a well-labeled planetary vorticity vector \(f\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(1.4586 \times 10^{-4}\)
    The vorticity of the fluid motion relative to Earth’s surface is called the relative vorticity. It is calculated from the water velocities relative to Earth’s surface (which is rotating). The total vorticity of a piece of fluid is the sum of the relative vorticity and planetary vorticity. The total vorticity is sometimes called absolute vorticity, because it is the vorticity the fluid has in the non-rotating reference frame of the stars.
    In large-scale dynamic meterology, we are in general concerned only with the vertical components of absolute and relative vorticity, which are designated by \(\eta\) and \(\zeta\text{,}\) respectively.
    \begin{align*} \zeta = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k} \cdot \nu \\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{v}) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k} \cdot (\nabla \times (u\vec{i} + v\vec{j} + w\vec{k})) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \end{align*}
    Since \(\eta = \vec{k} \cdot \vec{\nu}_{abs} = \vec{k} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{v}_{abs})\text{,}\) we have \(\eta = \zeta + f\) where \(f\) is planetary vorticity.

    Example 6.3.10. Estimating Vorticity of a Horizontal Wind.

    In Example 6.2.10, we computed a negative circulation \(\Gamma\) for a 1000 km \(\times\) 1000 km square where the horizontal wind \(\vec{v}\) was easterly and decreasing at a rate of \(10 \frac{m}{s}\) per 1000 km to the north. That is, \(\vec{v}=-(20-y/100 \frac{m}{s})\vec{i}\) (see Figure 6.3.11). Is there vorticity at the center of this square?

    Checkpoint 6.3.12. Estimate \(\eta\).

    The horizontal wind vector field near a given location is illustrated in Figure 6.3.13. The square is \(100 \ m\) by \(100 \ m\) and each vector has magnitude \(10 \ \frac{m}{s} \text{.}\) Numerically estimate the vertical component \(\eta = \nabla \times \vec{v}\) of vorticity. Recall that \(\displaystyle \eta = \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    We estimate \(\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta x} \approx \frac{0-10 \ \frac{m}{s}}{100 \ m} = -0.1 \ \frac{1}{s}\) and \(\frac{\Delta u}{\Delta y} \approx \frac{10\cos(45)-10\cos(60) \ \frac{m}{s}}{100 \ m} \approx -0.02 \ \frac{1}{s}\text{.}\) It follows that the vertical component of vorticity is \(\eta = \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \approx -0.1 - 0.02 = -0.12 \ \frac{1}{s}\text{.}\)

    6. Estimating the Vertical Component of Vorticity.

    In Checkpoint 6.2.12, we determined the circulation \(\Gamma\) about a \(300 \ km \times 200 \ km\) rectangle where the (wind) velocity vector \(\vec{v}\) was easterly and decreasing linearly at a rate of \(2.5 \frac{m}{s}\) per \(100 \ km\) toward the north. That is, \(\vec{v} = -(10-y/40\frac{m}{s})\vec{i} \text{.}\) Estimate now the vertical component of vorticity (\(\nabla \times \vec{v}\)) at the center of the rectangle.
    Answer.
    Since \(\vec{v} = u\vec{i} + v\vec{j} + w\vec{k} = -(10-y/40 \frac{m}{s})\vec{i}\text{,}\) \(u = -10+y/40 \frac{m}{s}\) and so \(\displaystyle \eta = \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -1/40 \frac{1}{s}\text{.}\)

    7. Curl of the Gradient is Zero.

    For scalar functions \(f(x,y,z)\) that are continuously twice-differentiable (i.e. ’nice’), the second mixed partial derivatives are always equal. For example,
    \begin{gather*} \displaystyle \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right). \end{gather*}
    Use this fact to demonstrate that the curl of a gradient vector field is always the zero vector. That is, show
    \begin{gather*} \nabla \times (\nabla f) = \vec{0}. \end{gather*}
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} \nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathstrut \amp det\left( \begin{array}{ccc}\vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \amp \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \amp \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \end{array}\right)\\ = \mathstrut \amp \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)\vec{i} - \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)\vec{j} \\ \mathstrut \amp + \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)\vec{k}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{0}. \end{align*}
