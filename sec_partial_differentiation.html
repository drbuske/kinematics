    Section 5.1 Partial Differentiation

    Motivating Questions

    • How do we measure the instantaneous rate of change of a function \(f(x,y)\) with respect to a change in an independent variable \(x\) or \(y\text{?}\)
    • Given a function \(f(x,y)\text{,}\) how do we compute or estimate the first-order partial derivatives \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) and what do they tell us about \(f\text{?}\)
    Suppose that \(T(x,y)\) represents the surface temperature (measured in degrees Fahrenheit) as a function of longitude \(x\) (measured in degrees east of the prime meridian) and latitude \(y\) (measured in degrees north of the equator). How does surface temperature \(T\) change with respect to a small change in longitude \(x\text{?}\) How does \(T\) change with respect to a small change in latitude \(y\text{?}\)
    In many circumstances, we are interested in a rate of change of a dependent variable with respect to an independent variable on which it depends. For example, at what rate does the wind chill \(C(v,T)\) change at an air temperature of \(T=-10^{\circ}F\) when the wind speed \(v\) increases by 1 mph? At any given instant, this value is a derivative. In what follows, we attempt to understand such derivatives graphically, numerically, and algebraically.

    Subsection 5.1.1 First-order Partial Derivatives

    Example 5.1.2. Geometric Idea of the Partial Derivative.

    Suppose we are located on the surface \(z=9-x^2-y^2\) at \((1,2,4)\text{.}\) As your \(x\) or \(y\) coordinate changes, the value of \(z\) will also change. That is, the output of the function \(f(x,y) = 9-x^2 - y^2\text{,}\) representing your height on the surface, changes as your input, an ordered pair \((x,y)\text{,}\) changes. If the input is \((1,2)\text{,}\) the output is \(z=4\text{.}\) So how fast does the \(z\)-value, or height on the surface, change if we increase the \(x\)-value of your location while holding the \(y\) value constant at 2? That is, as we walk along the surface in the positive \(x\)-direction?
    The surface \(z=9-x^2-y^2\) is shown in Figure 5.1.3. We will restrict our movement to the plane \(y=2\text{.}\) The intersection of these two surfaces is a space curve - the parabola \(z=5-x^2\) parallel to the \(xz-\)plane. The slope of this curve at \((1,2,4)\) is the same as the slope of the parabola \(z=5-x^2\) at \((1,4)\text{.}\) Using calculus, thd slope of this parabola at \((x_0,z_0)=(1,4)\) is \(\frac{dz}{dx}=-2\text{.}\) This means that at the point \((1,2,4)\) on the surface, our rate of change in height, \(z\text{,}\) is \(-2\) as we change \(x\text{.}\) We denote this fact by writing \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,2) = -2\) and saying that the partial derivative of \(f(x,y)\) with respect to \(x\) at \((1,2)\) is \(-2\text{.}\)

    Example 5.1.9. Algebraic Computation of the Partial Derivative.

    Once again we are interested in knowing how fast the dependent variable \(z\) changes with respect to a change in the independent variable \(y\) when \(z = f(x,y) = 9-x^2-y^2\text{.}\) Further, we are interested in this change at the moment that \(x=1, y=2\text{,}\) and \(z=4\text{.}\) We limit our interest to the rate as \(y\) changes; we hold the value of \(x\) constant at \(x=1\text{.}\) We evaluate the difference quotient
    \begin{align*} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{f(x,y+\Delta y)-f(x,y)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{(9-x^2-(y+\Delta y)^2) - (9-x^2-y^2)}{\Delta y}. \end{align*}
    When we then fix \(x=1\) and let \(\Delta y \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) the partial derivative emerges. That is,
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(1,2) \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{f(1,y+\Delta y)-f(1,y)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{(8-(y+\Delta y)^2) - (8-y^2)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2y. \end{align*}
    When \(y=2\text{,}\) we see that \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(1,2)=-4\text{.}\) Compare this result to those in Checkpoint 5.1.4 and Example 5.1.6. Also, note how closely this calculation resembles that of computing \(g'(2)\) when \(g(y) = 8-y^2\text{.}\)

    Checkpoint 5.1.10. Algebraic Computation of \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\).

    For \(z=f(x,y)=9-x^2-y^2\text{,}\) evaluate \(\lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta x}\) to find \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,2)\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    We evaluate the difference quotient
    \begin{align*} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta x} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{f(x+\Delta x,y)-f(x,y)}{\Delta x} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{(9-(x+\Delta x)^2-y^2) - (9-x^2-y^2)}{\Delta x}. \end{align*}
    When we then fix \(y=2\) and let \(\Delta x \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) we find the partial derivative \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,2)\text{.}\) That is,
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,2) = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta x}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x,2)-f(x,2)}{\Delta x} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{(5-(x+\Delta x)^2) - (5-x^2)}{\Delta x} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2x. \end{align*}
    When \(x=1\text{,}\) we see that \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,2)=-2\text{.}\) Compare this result to Example 5.1.2 and Checkpoint 5.1.8.
    The computations in Example 5.1.9 and Checkpoint 5.1.10 illustrate the formal definition of a partial derivative.

    Definition 5.1.11. Partial Derivative.

    Let \(f\) be a function of two variables \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) The partial derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) at \((a,b)\text{,}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a,b) = \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(a+\Delta x,b)-f(a,b)}{\Delta x} \end{equation*}
    provided it exists.
    Similarly, when the limit exists,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(a,b) = \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(a,b+\Delta y)-f(a,b)}{\Delta y}. \end{equation*}
    If we let \(a\) and \(b\) vary in Definition 5.1.11, we have partial derivative functions \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y)\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x,y)\text{.}\) Alternatively, if \(z = f(x,y)\text{,}\) we sometimes write \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y) = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y) = \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\text{.}\)
    Since the partial derivative \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y)\) is the ordinary derivative of the function \(f(x,y)\) with \(y\) held constant and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x,y)\) is the ordinary derivative of \(f(x,y)\) with \(x\) held constant, we can use all the differentiation formulas from one-variable calculus to find partial derivatives.

    Example 5.1.12.

    \(f(x,y) = 5xy+ y^2\text{,}\)\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y) = 5y\)\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x,y) = 5x + 2y\text{.}\)\(z = 5xy+y^2\text{,}\)\(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 5y\)\(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = 5x+2y\text{.}\)\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y) = 5y\text{,}\)\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(0,1) = 5\text{,}\)\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(2,1) = 5\text{,}\)\(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(2,-2) = -10\text{.}\)

    Checkpoint 5.1.13. Bulk Richardson Number.

    In the context of convective storm forecasting, the Bulk Richardson Number abbreviated as BRN is a nondimensional ratio of the convective available potential energy (CAPE) to a measure of the vertical wind shear (\(\frac{1}{2}U^2\)). The BRN is used to characterize convective-storm types for various environments.
    As a function of two variables, we write
    \begin{equation*} {\rm{BRN}}({\rm{CAPE}},U) = \frac{\rm{CAPE}}{\frac{1}{2}U^2} \end{equation*}
    where CAPE represents buoyant energy and \(U\) represents the difference between the 6-km density-weighted mean wind speed and a 500-m mean surface layer wind speed. A graph of this function appears in Figure 5.1.15.
    1. Is BRN an increasing or decreasing function of the variable \(U\text{?}\)
    2. Suppose that CAPE = 2500 J/kg and \(U=11\) m/s. What ordered triple does this correspond to on the surface graphed above? Should we expect multicellular thunderstorm development?
    3. Based on the surface given, should we expect \(\frac{\partial {\rm{(BRN)}}}{\partial U}\) to be positive, negative, or zero? Why?
    4. Using your differentiation skills, compute \(\frac{\partial {\rm{(BRN)}}}{\partial U}\text{.}\) Is this value positive, negative, or zero?
    Answer.
    1. Decreasing
    2. \(\left(2500,11,\frac{5000}{121}\right)\) or roughly \((5000,11,41.3)\) which is associated with supercell development (not multicellular thunderstorm development).
    3. Negative. If the surface is intersected with a vertical plane CAPE=constant, the resulting curve will be decreasing as \(U\) increases. That is, it has a negative slope.
    4. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial {\rm{(BRN)}}}{\partial U} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial U} 2{\rm{(BRN)}}U^{-2}\\ = \mathstrut \amp -4{\rm{(BRN)}}U^{-3} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -4 \cdot \frac{\rm{BRN}}{U^3}\\ < \mathstrut \amp 0 \end{align*}

    Subsection 5.1.2 Interpretations of First-order Partial Derivatives

    Recall that the derivative of a single variable function has a geometric interpretation as the slope of the line tangent to the graph at a given point. Similarly, we have seen that the partial derivatives measure the slope of a line tangent to a trace of a function of two variables as shown in Figure 5.1.16.
    Now we consider the first-order partial derivatives in context. Recall that the difference quotient \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) for a function \(f\) of a single variable \(x\) at a point where \(x=a\) tells us the average rate of change of \(f\) over the interval \([a,a+h]\text{,}\) while the derivative \(f'(a)\) tells us the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) at \(x=a\text{.}\) We can use these same concepts to explain the meanings of the partial derivatives in context.

    Example 5.1.17. Launching a Projectile.

    Consider the function \(f\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y) = \frac{x^2 \sin(2 y)}{32}, \end{equation*}
    which measures the range, or horizontal distance, in feet, traveled by a projectile launched with an initial speed of \(x\) feet per second at an angle \(y\) radians to the horizontal. The graph of this function is given on the left in Figure 5.1.18. Moreover, if we fix the angle \(y = 0.6\text{,}\) we may view the trace \(f(x,0.6)\) as a function of \(x\) alone, as seen at right in Figure 5.1.18.
    Note that
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(150,0.6) = \mathstrut \amp \left. \frac{d}{dx}[f(x,0.6)]\right\rvert_{x=150}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \left. \frac{2x\sin(1.2)}{32} \right\rvert_{x=150}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\sin(1.2)}{32}300 \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 8.74~\mbox{feet per feet per second} , \end{align*}
    which gives the slope of the tangent line shown on the right of Figure 5.1.18. Thinking of this derivative as an instantaneous rate of change implies that if we increase the initial speed of the projectile by one foot per second (from 150 feet per second to 151 feet per second), we should expect the horizontal distance traveled to increase by approximately 8.74 feet if we hold the launch angle constant at \(0.6\) radians.
    In the same way, we may obtain a trace by setting, say, \(x=150\) as shown in Figure 5.1.19.
    This gives
    \begin{equation*} f(150, y) = \frac{150^2}{32}\sin(2y), \end{equation*}
    and therefore
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(150,0.6) = \mathstrut \amp \left. \frac{d}{dy}[f(150,y)]\right\rvert_{y=0.6} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \left. \frac{150^2}{16}\cos(2y)\right\rvert_{y=0.6}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{150^2}{16}\cos(1.2) \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 509.5 ~\mbox{feet per radian}. \end{align*}
    Once again, the derivative gives the slope of the tangent line shown on the right in Figure 5.1.19. Thinking of the derivative as an instantaneous rate of change, we expect that the range of the projectile increases by 509.5 feet for every radian we increase the launch angle \(y\) if we keep the initial speed of the projectile constant at 150 feet per second.

    Checkpoint 5.1.20. Interpreting the Meaning of a Wind Chill Partial.

    Let \(C = f(w,T)\) where \(C\) is the wind chill temperature (in degrees Fahrenheit) that is associated with a wind speed of \(w\) miles per hour and a temperature of \(T\) degrees Fahrenheit. A table of values for the function \(f\) is given in Table 5.1.21.
    1. Estimate \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(10,25)\text{.}\) What does your answer mean in practical terms?
    2. Estimate \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial T}(5,20)\text{.}\) What does your answer mean in practical terms?
    3. When the temperature is \(20^{\circ}F\text{,}\) the temperature adjusted for wind chill drops by an average of about \(0.8^{\circ}F\) with every 1 mph increase in wind speed from 5 mph to 10 mph. Which partial derivative is this telling you about?

    1. \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(10,25) \approx \frac{13-19}{15-5} = -0.6 \frac{^{\circ}F}{\rm{mph}}\text{;}\) in practical terms, this means that when the wind is blowing at 10 mph and the temperature is \(25^{\circ}F\text{,}\) then it will feel roughly -0.6 degrees colder for every 1 mph increase in wind speed.     2. \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial T}(5,20) \approx \frac{19-7}{25-15} = 1.2\frac{^{\circ}F}{^{\circ}F} = 1.2\text{;}\) in practical terms, when the wind is blowing at 5 mph and the temperature is \(20^{\circ}F\text{,}\) then it will feel roughly 1.2 degrees Fahrenheit warmer for every 1 degree Fahrenheit the temperature increases - again a rate of change.
    3. This suggests that \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(5,20)\) and \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(10,20)\) are both approximately \(-0.8 \frac{^{\circ}F}{\rm{mph}}\text{.}\)

    9. Relating Rates of Change in Coordinate Systems.

    We know that the rectangular coordinates \((x,y)\) of a point \(P\) are related to the polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) of \(P\) by the equations
    \begin{align*} x = \mathstrut \amp r\cos\theta\\ y = \mathstrut \amp r\sin\theta \end{align*}
    Find \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial r}\) and \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\) and interpret the meaning of these partial derivatives.
    Answer.
    \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial r} = \cos\theta\) gives the rate at which the \(x\)-coordinate of point \(P\) changes as a small change is made to its distance \(r\) from the origin. \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta} = -r\sin\theta\) gives the rate at which the \(x\)-coordinate of point \(P\) changes as a small change is made to the angle \(\theta\) the point makes with the positive \(x\)-axis.

    10. Mean Wind Speed.

    The mean wind speed \(M\) is a function of \(U\text{,}\) the eastward wind speed, and \(V\text{,}\) the northward wind speed. In fact,
    \begin{equation*} M = \sqrt{U^2 + V^2}. \end{equation*}
    1. If the eastward wind speed is \(U=3\) m/sec and the northward wind speed is \(V=4\) m/sec, determine the mean wind speed \(M\text{.}\)
    2. Compute \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial U}\) when \(U = 3\) m/sec and \(V = 4\) m/sec. Be sure the state units with your response.
    3. Interpret the meaning of \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial U}(3,4)\) computed in (b).
    Answer.
    1. 5 m/sec
    2. \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial U} = \frac{U}{\sqrt{U^2 + V^2}}\) so that \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial U}(3,4)= \frac{3}{3^2 + 4^2} = \frac{3}{5}\) which is unitless. To see this, note that units are those of \(M\) over those of \(U\) or (m/sec)/(m/sec).
    3. \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial U}(3,4)\) is the rate at which the mean wind speed is changing with respect to changes in the eastward wind speed at the moment the eastward wind speed is 3 m/sec and the northward wind speed is 4 m/sec.

    11. Saturated Vapor Pressure.

    The saturated vapor pressure, \(S(T,R)\) (in millibars) is the difference between the theoretical pressure exerted by the water vapor held in saturated air (100% relative humidity \(R\) at a given temperature \(T\) in degrees Celsius) and the pressure exerted by the water vapor that is actually held in the air being measured at the same given temperature.

    13. Potential Temperature.

    The potential temperature of a parcel of air at pressure \(p\)p (in mb) and absolute temperature \(T\) (in \(K\)) is the temperature \(\theta\) that the parcel would attain if adiabatically brought to a standard pressure of 1000 mb. In particular,
    \begin{equation*} \theta(T,p) = T \left( \frac{1000}{p} \right)^{0.286}. \end{equation*}
    1. Compute \(\theta(280,1000)\text{.}\) What units does this value have?
    2. When \(p < 1000\) (as it is in the atmosphere) is the potential temperature \(\theta\) greater or less than the absolute temperature \(T\text{?}\) What happens as \(p\) decreases (as a rising air parcel does in the atmosphere)?
    3. Compute \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial T}(T,p)\text{.}\) What does this calculation say about how the potential temperature changes when holding pressure \(p\) constant?
    4. Compute \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial p}(T,p)\text{.}\) What does it mean to have this value be negative?
    Answer.
    1. \(\displaystyle \theta(280,1000) = 280 \left( \frac{1000}{1000} \right)^{0.286} = 280 \ K\)
    2. When \(p < 1000\text{,}\) the base of the exponential part of the function \(\theta(T,p)\) is greater than 1 leading to a potential temperature that is greater than the absolute temperature. That is, \(\theta > T\text{.}\) As \(p\) decreases, the potential temperature \(\theta\) increases.
    3. \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial T}(T,p) = \left( \frac{1000}{p} \right)^{0.286}\) so that for every one degree increase in absolute temperature \(T\text{,}\) the potential temperature \(\theta\) increases by \(\left( \frac{1000}{p} \right)^{0.286}\text{.}\) Note that this amount of increase does not depend on the initial temperature.
    4. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial p}(T,p) = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial p} (1000)^{0.286} p^{-0.286} T \\ = \mathstrut \amp (-0.286)(1000)^{0.286} p^{-1.286} T\\ = \mathstrut \amp -2.06237 \left( \frac{1}{p} \right)^{1.286} T \end{align*}
      Being negative, this means that when holding the absolute temperature constant but increasing the pressure of an air parcel (by, say, having it drop to a lower height in the atmosphere), the potential temperature decreases (and does so at this calculated rate for this temperature \(T\) and pressure \(p\)).

    16. Wet Bulb Globe Temperature.

    The wet bulb globe temperature, or WBGT, is an envrionmental monitoring measure introduced in the early 1950’s in response to heat casualties occurring in the United States armed forces. WBGT utilizes ambient temperature, relative humidity, wind, and solar radiation from the sun to get a composite value that can be used when monitoring environmental conditions during exercise in the heat. More specifically,
    \begin{equation*} {\rm{WBGT}}(T_w, T_g, T_d) = 0.7T_w + 0.2T_g + 0.1T_d \end{equation*}
    where \(T_w\) is the wet bulb temperature (indicating humidity), \(T_g\) is the globe temperature (indicating radiant heat), and \(T_d\) is the ambient air (dry) temperature. Here, each of \(T_w, T_g\text{,}\) and \(T_d\) are measured in degrees Celsius.
    1. In what units is WBGT measured?
    2. Calculate \(\frac{\partial {\rm{WBGT}}}{\partial T_w}\text{.}\) Be sure to include units on this value. Then, interpret this value’s meaning.
    3. In some instances when a WBGT monitoring device is unavailable, a chart as in Figure 5.1.29 can be used to estimate WBGT. These are estimates and are derived only from using temperature \(T\) (in \(^{\circ}C\)) and relative humidity \(RH\) (as a percent) and the chart accounts for full sunshine and light wind conditions. Depending on the radiant heat load from the sun and the wind, the actual WBGT reading could be different from what is on the chart.
      The chart gives \({\rm{WBGT}}(T,RH)\text{.}\) Use a central difference to estimate the value of \(\frac{\partial{\rm{WBGT}}}{\partial T}(30,95)\text{.}\) Be sure include units. Then, interpret the meaning of this value.
    Answer.
    1. Degrees Celsius
    2. \(\frac{\partial {\rm{WBGT}}}{\partial T_w} = 0.7 \frac{^{\circ}C}{^{\circ}C}\text{.}\) This value tells us that for each 1 degree increase in the wet bulb temperature, with both \(T_g\) and \(T_d\) held constant, we can expect a \(0.7^{\circ}C\) increase in WBGT.
    3. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial{\rm{WBGT}}}{\partial T}(30,95) \approx \mathstrut \amp \frac{38^{\circ}C - 35^{\circ}C}{31^{\circ}C - 29^{\circ}C}\\ = \mathstrut \amp 1.5\frac{^{\circ}C}{^{\circ}C} \end{align*}
