    Section 5.3 The Chain Rule

    Motivating Questions

    • What is the multivariate chain rule and how do we use it to find a derivative?
    • How can we use a tree diagram to guide us in implementing the chain rule?
    In one-variable calculus, Example 5.3.1 describes the chain rule. Due to variation in notation you may not recognize it, but the main idea is the multiplication of rates in the context of function composition.

    Example 5.3.1. A simple race.

    If Sally runs twice as fast as Patty and Patty runs five times as fast as Joey, then Sally runs ten times faster than Joey. Mathematically, if \(S\) = Sally’s position, \(P\) = Patty’s position, and \(J\) = Joey’s position, then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta S}{\Delta J} = \frac{\Delta S}{\Delta P} \cdot \frac{\Delta P}{\Delta J} \end{equation*}
    Another way to write this would be using derivatives:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dS}{dJ} = \left(\frac{dS}{dP} \right) \left(\frac{dP}{dJ} \right) = (2)(5)=10 . \end{equation*}
    In a multivariable setting, we first multiply related rates. Then, we must add the contributions from the unrelated variables. One useful way to understand how this works is to think about a function as a map from one space to another rather than as a graph (see Figure 5.3.3). The derivative is no longer the slope; it is now the amount by which the function locally gets squashed/stretched. This squash/stretch factor is \(\frac{df}{dx} \approx \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}\text{.}\)
    In this context, the chain rule then says to get the total amount of stretching (or squashing), just multiply the stretch factors. Figure 5.3.4 shows how \(\frac{df}{dx} = \frac{df}{dg} \cdot \frac{dg}{dx}\) since \(\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} =\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta g} \cdot \frac{\Delta g}{\Delta x}\text{.}\)

    Example 5.3.5. The Chain Rule for \(P = f(x,y)\text{,}\) \(x=g(t)\text{,}\) \(y=h(t)\).

    Suppose that \(P = f(x,y)\text{,}\) \(x=g(t)\text{,}\) \(y=h(t)\text{.}\) We see how
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = \frac{\partial{P}}{\partial{x}} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial{P}}{\partial{y}} \frac{dy}{dt} \end{equation*}
    from the diagram in Figure 5.3.6.
    When thinking about the chain rule, it is sometimes helpful to think of the food chain. In this case, think of \(P\) as representing the population of large fish that feeds on two types of medium fish having populations \(x\) and \(y\) respectively. The populations of these medium fish depends on the population of small fish \(t\text{.}\) In this way, it is easy to see how variation in the population of small fish impacts the populations \(x\) and \(y\) of medium fish which, in turn, impacts the population \(P\) of large fish. The chain rule is the means by which we measure the rate at which the population \(P\) of large fish changes with respect to a change in the population \(t\) of small fish. Note how the resulting rate of change in this case, \(\frac{dP}{dt}\text{,}\) is an ordinary derivative and not a partial derivative. Indeed the population of large fish ultimately only depends on a single variable \(t\text{,}\) the population of small fish.

    Example 5.3.7. A Warming Path.

    Suppose the temperature (in \(^{\circ}C\)) is given by the function \(T(x,y)\) whose contours are shown in Figure 5.3.8 (\(x, y\) are in kilometers). If you consider how your location corresponds to points in the \(xy\)-plane, you know that when you pass the point \((2,1)\text{,}\) your velocity vector is \(-\vec{i} + 2\vec{j}\) (km/hr). We estimate the rate of change of your temperature, \(\frac{dT}{dt}\text{,}\) when passing through \((2,1)\text{.}\)
    First, note that the dependence diagram from Example 5.3.5 with \(T\) replacing variable \(P\) applies here.
    We have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dT}{dt} = \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{x}} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{y}} \frac{dy}{dt}. \end{equation*}
    Next, note that when \(x = 2\) and \(y=-1\text{,}\) the velocity vector tells us that \(\frac{dx}{dt} = -1\) and \(\frac{dy}{dt} = 2\text{.}\) Using the contours, we can estimate \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \approx \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta x}\) and \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial y} \approx \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta y}\) to obtain
    \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{x}} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{y}} \frac{dy}{dt} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp \left( \frac{2-4}{3-1.5} \right)\frac{^{\circ}C}{km} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + \left( \frac{4-2}{1.75-0.25} \right)\frac{^{\circ}C}{km} \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp (-1.33 ^{\circ}C/km)\left(-1 \ \frac{km}{hr}\right) + (1.33 ^{\circ}C/km)\left(2 \ \frac{km}{hr}\right) \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 4 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr}. \end{align*}
    This tells us that as we move in the direction of velocity vector at \((2,1)\text{,}\) the temperature will rise (which is clear from the contour map). But it also tells us how quickly the temperature will rise. Moreover, you can see the contributions to this rate of change that come from our movement in the horizontal direction (1.33 \(^{\circ}C/hr\)) and our movement in the vertical direction (2.66 \(^{\circ}C/hr\)). We warm as we move west, but we warm even faster, roughly twice as fast, as we move north.

    Checkpoint 5.3.10. A Dropping Wind Chill.

    The wind chill index is modeled by the function
    \begin{equation*} W(T,v) = 13.12 + 0.6215T - 11.37v^{0.16} + 0.3965Tv^{0.16} \end{equation*}
    where \(T\) is the temperature in degrees Celsius and \(v\) is the wind speed (in km/hr). The temperature \(T\) and the wind speed \(v\) are also, of course, functions of time \(t\text{.}\) Suppose that at 11:00 a.m., we have \(T = -15^{\circ}C\) and \(v = 30\) km/hr. Further, suppose that \(T(t)\) is decreasing by \(^{\circ}C/hr\) and \(v(t)\) is increasing by 2 km/hr. That is, \(\frac{dT}{dt} = -4 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr}\) and \(\frac{dv}{dt} = 2 \ \frac{km/hr}{hr}\text{.}\) At what rate is the apparent temperature (i.e. the wind chill \(W\)) changing at 11:00 a.m.?
    Answer.
    By the chain rule, note that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dW}{dt} = \frac{\partial W}{\partial T}\frac{dT}{dt} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial v}\frac{dv}{dt}. \end{equation*}
    Since
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial W}{\partial T}= 0.6215 + 0.3965v^{0.16} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial W}{\partial v}= (0.16)(11.37)v^{-0.84} + (0.16)(0.3965)Tv^{-0.84} \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial W}{\partial T}(-15,30) = \mathstrut \amp 0.6215+0.3965(30)^{0.16}\\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 1.305 \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial W}{\partial v}(-15,30) = \mathstrut \amp (0.16)(11.37)(30)^{-0.84} + (0.16)(0.3965)(-15)(30)^{-0.84}\\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 0.05. \end{align*}
    So at 11:00 a.m. (when \(T = -15^{\circ}C\) and \(v = 30\) km/hr) we have
    \begin{align*} \frac{dW}{dt} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial W}{\partial T}\frac{dT}{dt} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial v}\frac{dv}{dt} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp \left(1.305 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{^{\circ}C} \right) \left( -4 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr}\right) + \left( 0.05 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{km/hr} \right) \left( 2 \ \frac{km/hr}{hr}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp -5.22 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr} + 0.1 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -5.12 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr}. \end{align*}
    This means that the wind chill (or feels like temperature) is dropping at a rate of 5.12 degrees Celsius per hour at 11:00 a.m.. Most of the contribution to this drop is coming from the rapid drop in ambient temperature \(T\text{.}\)

    9. A Changing Heat Index.

    Both relative humidity \(R\) and temperature \(T\) are functions of location. That is, \(R=R(x,y)\) and \(T=T(x,y)\text{.}\) Using the chain rule, complete the dependence diagram in Figure 5.3.11 and write out the formula for \(\frac{\partial H}{\partial x}\text{,}\) the rate at which the heat index \(H = H(R,T)\) changes due to a small movement to the east. Hint: Your answer will involve other partial derivatives.
    Answer.
    \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial H}{\partial x} =\frac{\partial H}{\partial T}\cdot \frac{\partial T}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial H}{\partial R}\cdot \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}\)

    10. The Transport Equation.

    The transport equation models the concentration of a substance flowing in a fluid at a constant rate. In one dimension, one example of a transport equation is given by
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + c \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 0 \end{equation*}
    where \(t\) represents time, \(x\) represents displacement from a fixed point, and \(u(x,t)\) is a function of two variables representing the concentration of the substances at location \(x\) and time \(t\text{.}\)
    1. Suppose \(u(x,t) = e^{x-ct}\text{.}\) Compute \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\text{.}\) Then verify that \(u(x,t)\) satisfies the above transport equation.
    2. Repeat (a) using \(u(x,t) = \sin(x-ct)\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    1. \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = -ce^{x-ct}\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = e^{x-ct}\) so that \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+c\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = -ce^{x-ct} + c e^{x-ct} = 0\text{.}\)
    2. \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = -c \cos(x-ct)\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} =\cos(x-ct)\) so that \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+c\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = -c\cos(x-ct) + c \cos(x-ct) = 0\text{.}\)

    11. Advection and the Chain Rule.

    Suppose that \(Q = Q(t, x, y, z)\) is a differentiable function of \(t, x, y\text{,}\) and \(z\text{,}\) where \(x, y\text{,}\) and \(z\) are all differentiable functions of \(t\text{.}\) Draw a dependence diagram and write out the corresponding formula(s) for the Chain Rule. If \(\vec{v} = \frac{dx}{dt} \vec{i} + \frac{dy}{dt} \vec{j} + \frac{dz}{dt} \vec{k}\text{,}\) show that \(\frac{dQ}{dt}\) can be written as \(\frac{\partial{Q}}{\partial{t}} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla Q\text{.}\) Recall: \(-\vec{v} \cdot \nabla Q\) is called advection.
    Answer.
    By the chain rule, \(\displaystyle \frac{dQ}{dt} =\frac{\partial Q}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\cdot \frac{dx}{dt}+ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\cdot \frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\cdot \frac{dz}{dt}\text{.}\) But \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial{Q}}{\partial{t}} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla Q = \frac{\partial{Q}}{\partial{t}} + (\frac{dx}{dt} \vec{i} + \frac{dy}{dt} \vec{j} + \frac{dz}{dt} \vec{k})\cdot (\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\vec{i}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\vec{j}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\vec{k})\) which is exactly \(\displaystyle \frac{dQ}{dt}\) found earlier.

    12. Pressuring a Drone.

    Atmospheric pressure is a function of location \((x,y,z)\) in space and of time \(t\text{.}\) That is, \(p = p(x,y,z,t)\text{.}\) A drone is located at various locations \((x,y,z)\) at time \(t\text{.}\) That is, \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) are all functions of time \(t\) for the drone. Give an expression involving partial derivatives for the instantaneous rate of change of pressure \(\frac{dp}{dt}\) on this drone.
    Answer.
    By the chain rule, \(\displaystyle \frac{dp}{dt} = \frac{\partial p}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial p}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial p}{\partial z} \cdot \frac{dz}{dt}\)
