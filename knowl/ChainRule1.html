Example 3.3.4. The Chain Rule for \(P = f(x,y)\text{,}\) \(x=g(t)\text{,}\) \(y=h(t)\).

Suppose that \(P = f(x,y)\text{,}\) \(x=g(t)\text{,}\) \(y=h(t)\text{.}\) We see how
\begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = \frac{\partial{P}}{\partial{x}} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial{P}}{\partial{y}} \frac{dy}{dt} \end{equation*}
from the diagram in Figure 3.3.5.
When thinking about the chain rule, it is sometimes helpful to think of the food chain. In this case, think of \(P\) as representing the population of large fish that feeds on two types of medium fish having populations \(x\) and \(y\) respectively. The populations of these medium fish depends on the population of small fish \(t\text{.}\) In this way, it is easy to see how variation in the population of small fish impacts the populations \(x\) and \(y\) of medium fish which, in turn, impacts the population \(P\) of large fish. The chain rule is the means by which we measure the rate at which the population \(P\) of large fish changes with respect to a change in the population \(t\) of small fish. Note how the resulting rate of change in this case, \(\frac{dP}{dt}\text{,}\) is an ordinary derivative and not a partial derivative. Indeed the population of large fish ultimately only depends on a single variable \(t\text{,}\) the population of small fish.
