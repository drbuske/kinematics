To calculate \(\vec{i} \times (\vec{i} + \vec{j})\text{,}\) we start with sketching both vectors as shown in FigureÂ 2.3.9. Vectors \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\) form a parallelogram that lives in the \(xy\)-plane. In fact, this parallelogram is actually a 1 by 1 square. The area of this parallelogram is 1. Since the unit vector \(\vec{k}\) is perpendicular to both, we have \(\vec{i} \times (\vec{i} + \vec{j}) = 1 \cdot \vec{k}\text{.}\) Note that \(-\vec{k}\) is also perpendicular to both \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\text{.}\) However, by the right-hand rule, since \(\vec{i}\) is being crossed into \(\vec{i} + \vec{j}\text{,}\) the cross product will point in the positive \(\vec{k}\) direction. Note though that \((\vec{i} + \vec{j}) \times \vec{i} = -\vec{k}\) by the right-hand rule.
