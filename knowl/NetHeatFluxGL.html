Example 4.4.9. Net Heat Flux in the Great Lakes.

Figure 4.4.10 shows net heat flux, \(Q\text{,}\) into the lake surface of the Great Lakes during the month of January.
The net heat flux \(Q\) adds the influence of solar radiation to the sensible and latent heat fluxes. In particular, we have
\begin{gather*} Q = R_s + R_{dl} - R_{ul} - LE - H \end{gather*}
where \(R_s\) is the absorbed solar (shortwave) radiation, \(R_{dl}\) is the downward longwave radiation from the atmosphere and clouds, \(R_{ul}\) is the upward longwave radiation from the lake surface, and once again \(LE\) is the latent heat flux and \(H\) is the sensible heat flux.
Based on Figure 4.4.10, one might worry about the net negative heat flux \(Q\) in the Great Lakes during January. However, net fluxes are negative during the winter and positive during the summer (see Figure 4.4.11). Moreover, net heat flux is near zero on the surface of the Great Lakes when averaged over a typical year.
