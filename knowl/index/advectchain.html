Exercise 11. Advection and the Chain Rule.

Suppose that \(Q = Q(t, x, y, z)\) is a differentiable function of \(t, x, y\text{,}\) and \(z\text{,}\) where \(x, y\text{,}\) and \(z\) are all differentiable functions of \(t\text{.}\) Draw a dependence diagram and write out the corresponding formula(s) for the Chain Rule. If \(\vec{v} = \frac{dx}{dt} \vec{i} + \frac{dy}{dt} \vec{j} + \frac{dz}{dt} \vec{k}\text{,}\) show that \(\frac{dQ}{dt}\) can be written as \(\frac{\partial{Q}}{\partial{t}} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla Q\text{.}\) Recall: \(-\vec{v} \cdot \nabla Q\) is called advection.
Answer.
By the chain rule, \(\displaystyle \frac{dQ}{dt} =\frac{\partial Q}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\cdot \frac{dx}{dt}+ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\cdot \frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\cdot \frac{dz}{dt}\text{.}\) But \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial{Q}}{\partial{t}} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla Q = \frac{\partial{Q}}{\partial{t}} + (\frac{dx}{dt} \vec{i} + \frac{dy}{dt} \vec{j} + \frac{dz}{dt} \vec{k})\cdot (\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\vec{i}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\vec{j}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\vec{k})\) which is exactly \(\displaystyle \frac{dQ}{dt}\) found earlier.
