Exercise 10. The Transport Equation.

The transport equation models the concentration of a substance flowing in a fluid at a constant rate. In one dimension, one example of a transport equation is given by
\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + c \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 0 \end{equation*}
where \(t\) represents time, \(x\) represents displacement from a fixed point, and \(u(x,t)\) is a function of two variables representing the concentration of the substances at location \(x\) and time \(t\text{.}\)
  1. Suppose \(u(x,t) = e^{x-ct}\text{.}\) Compute \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\text{.}\) Then verify that \(u(x,t)\) satisfies the above transport equation.
  2. Repeat (a) using \(u(x,t) = \sin(x-ct)\text{.}\)
Answer.
  1. \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = -ce^{x-ct}\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = e^{x-ct}\) so that \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+c\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = -ce^{x-ct} + c e^{x-ct} = 0\text{.}\)
  2. \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = -c \cos(x-ct)\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} =\cos(x-ct)\) so that \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+c\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = -c\cos(x-ct) + c \cos(x-ct) = 0\text{.}\)
