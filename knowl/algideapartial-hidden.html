Once again we are interested in knowing how fast the dependent variable \(z\) changes with respect to a change in the independent variable \(y\) when \(z = f(x,y) = 9-x^2-y^2\text{.}\) Further, we are interested in this change at the moment that \(x=1, y=2\text{,}\) and hence \(z=4\text{.}\) We limit our interest in the rate as \(y\) changes; we hold the value of \(x\) constant at \(x=1\text{.}\) As in calculus, we are interested in

\begin{align*} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{f(x,y+\Delta y)-f(x,y)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{(9-x^2-(y+\Delta y)^2) - (9-x^2-y^2)}{\Delta y} \end{align*}

where \(x\) is fixed at \(x=1\) and \(\Delta y \rightarrow 0\text{.}\) That is, we compute

\begin{align*} \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y} = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{f(1,y+\Delta y)-f(1,y)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{(8-(y+\Delta y)^2) - (8-y^2)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2y. \end{align*}

When \(y=2\text{,}\) this value is -4 (see Example 3.1.1 and~ Example 3.1.3). Also, note how closely this calculation resembles that of computing \(g'(2)\) when \(g(y) = 8-y^2\text{.}\)
