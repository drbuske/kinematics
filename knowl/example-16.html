Example 2.2.17. Two-Dimensional Advection.

FigureÂ 2.2.18 shows current temperatures observed near Rochester, MN. Suppose the horizontal wind vector is \(\vec{v}_H = (36 \ {\rm{km/hr}})\vec{i} -(11 \ {\rm{km/hr}})\vec{j}.\)
Here,
\begin{equation*} \vec{u}_T = \frac{(8.2-9.1)^{\circ}C}{153 \ {\rm{km}}} \vec{i} + \frac{(8.7-8.5)^{\circ}C}{56 \ {\rm{km}}}\vec{j}. \end{equation*}
It follows that the temperature advection at Rochester is
\begin{align*} -\vec{v}_H \cdot \vec{u}_T = \mathstrut \amp -\left[(36 \ {\rm{km/hr}})\vec{i} -(11 \ {\rm{km/hr}}) \vec{j}\right] \cdot \left(\frac{-0.9^{\circ}C}{153 \ {\rm{km}}} \vec{i} + \frac{0.2^{\circ}C}{56 \ {\rm{km}}}\vec{j}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{324}{1530} \frac{^{\circ}C}{\rm{hr}} + \frac{22}{560} \frac{^{\circ}C}{{\rm{hr}}} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 0.25\frac{^{\circ}C}{\rm{hr}} \end{align*}
If the current temperature at Rochester is \(8.5^{\circ}C\text{,}\) the temperature 2 hours from now is given by
\begin{align*} T_{fcst} = \mathstrut \amp T_{curr} + \Delta t(- \vec{v}_H \cdot \vec{u}_T)\\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 8.5^{\circ}C + (2 \ {\rm{hr}}) \left( 0.25\frac{^{\circ}C}{\rm{hr}}\right) \\ = \mathstrut \amp 9.0^{\circ}{C}\text{.} \end{align*}
