Vorticity.

Vorticity in fluids is similar to angular momentum in solids. Just like curl, it is easiest to visualize vorticity by thinking of a small paddle wheel immersed in the fluid. If the fluid flow turns the paddle wheel, then it has vorticity. Vorticity is a vector, and points out of the plane in which the fluid turns. The sign of the vorticity is given by the ``right-hand” rule. If you curl the fingers on your right hand in the direction of the turning paddle wheel and your thumb points upward, then the vorticity is positive. If your thumb points downward, the vorticity is negative. Does this sound familiar? It should! If not, review the concept of curl in the last section.

Definition 4.3.8. Vorticity.

Vorticity is a vector measure of local rotation in a fluid flow, defined as the curl of the velocity vector,
\begin{equation*} \nu = \nabla \times \vec{v}, \end{equation*}
where \(\nu\) is the vorticity, \(\vec{v}\) the velocity, and \(\nabla\) the gradient operator.
Vorticity, therefore, has units of inverse time, for instance, \(({\rm{sec}})^{-1}\text{.}\) Fluids (and all objects) have vorticity simply because of Earth's rotation. This is called planetary vorticity. We do not normally appreciate this component of vorticity since it is only important if a motion lasts for a significant portion of a day, and most important if it lasts for many days, months, or years. Since geostrophic motion is essentially steady compared with the rotation time of Earth, planetary vorticity is very important for nearly geostrophic flows. The vector of planetary vorticity \(f\) points upward, parallel to the rotation axis of Earth. Its magnitude is twice the angular rotation rate \(\Omega\) of Earth.

Checkpoint 4.3.9. Planetary Vorticity.

The vorticity of the fluid motion relative to Earth's surface is called the relative vorticity. It is calculated from the water velocities relative to Earth's surface (which is rotating). The total vorticity of a piece of fluid is the sum of the relative vorticity and planetary vorticity. The total vorticity is sometimes called absolute vorticity, because it is the vorticity the fluid has in the non-rotating reference frame of the stars.
In large-scale dynamic meterology, we are in general concerned only with the vertical components of absolute and relative vorticity, which are designated by \(\eta\) and \(\zeta\text{,}\) respectively.
\begin{align*} \zeta = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k} \cdot \nu \\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{v}) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \vec{k} \cdot (\nabla \times (u\vec{i} + v\vec{j} + w\vec{k})) \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \end{align*}
Since \(\eta = \vec{k} \cdot \vec{\nu}_{abs} = \vec{k} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{v}_{abs})\text{,}\) we have \(\eta = \zeta + f\) where \(f\) is planetary vorticity.
