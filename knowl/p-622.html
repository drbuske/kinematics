We estimate the divergence \(\nabla \cdot \vec{v}\) on the San Francisco coast at each level of the atmosphere.
  • At \(400 \ mb\text{,}\) \(\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} + 0 + 0 = \frac{0-60 \ km/hr}{1000 \ km} = -0.06 \frac{1}{hr}\text{.}\)
  • At \(300 \ mb\text{,}\) \(\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} = \frac{0-100 \ km/hr}{1000 \ km} = -0.1 \frac{1}{hr}\text{.}\)
  • At \(200 \ mb\text{,}\) \(\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} = \frac{0-180 \ km/hr}{1000 \ km} = -0.18 \frac{1}{hr}\text{.}\)
At each level of the atmosphere, the divergence is negative. That is, we have convergence.
