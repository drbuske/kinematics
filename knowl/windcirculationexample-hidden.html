Suppose the wind velocity vector field \(\vec{v}\) is easterly and decreasing at a rate of 10 m/s per 1000 km toward the north (see Figure 4.2.11). That is, \(\vec{v} = -(20-y/100)\vec{i}\) m/s. We determine the circulation \(\Gamma\) about a \(1000 \times 1000\) km square that is oriented in a counterclockwise fashion.

Since the contour \(C\) in this case is made up of 4 straight line segments, computation of the line integral giving \(\Gamma\) becomes a simple sum:

\begin{align*} \Gamma = \mathstrut \amp \int_C \vec{v} \cdot d\vec{r} \\ = \mathstrut \amp (-20 \ m/s)\vec{i} \cdot (1000 \ km)\vec{i} + (-(20-y/100) \ m/s)\vec{i} \cdot (1000 \ km)\vec{j} \\ \mathstrut \amp + (-10 \ m/s)\vec{i} \cdot (-1000 \ km)\vec{i} + (-(20-y/100) \ m/s)\vec{i} \cdot (-1000 \ km)\vec{j} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -20,000,000 \ m^2/s + 0 + 10,000,000 \ m^2/s + 0 \\ = \mathstrut \amp -1 \times 10^7 \ m^2/s. \end{align*}

It makes sense that \(\Gamma\) is negative - the wind is more against \(C\) than it is with \(C\text{.}\) Note also the units of the circulation are area per second.
