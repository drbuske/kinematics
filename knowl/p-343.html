Figure 2.3.3 better illustrates the top of the rotating disk in Figure 2.3.2. A mass moving with velocity vector \(\vec{v}\) is perpendicular to \(\vec{\omega}\) (which now points out of the screen). At any given time, the Coriolis force \(\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\) is perpendicular to both (a) the velocity vector \(\vec{v}\) and (b) the vector \(\vec{\omega}\text{.}\) Our goal in this section is to determine a means of calculating such a vector \(\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\) given \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{\omega}\text{.}\) That is, given two vectors \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{\omega}\text{,}\) we aim to find a third vector perpendicular to both whose magnitude increases in proportion to each. The cross product is a tool we can use to do this.
