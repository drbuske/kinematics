The values of \(z = 9-x^2-y^2\) near \(x=1\text{,}\) \(y=2\) are illustrated in Figure 3.1.4.

If we wish to estimate the rate at which \(z\) changes with respect to a change in \(y\) (while keeping \(x\) fixed at a value of 1), the table suggests to us that

\begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y} = \frac{3.59 - 4}{2.1 - 2} = -4.1. \end{equation*}

This is very similar to the instantaneous rate of change we calculated in Example 3.1.1. It should be noted too that other reasonable estimates on this rate can also be made from this table. For example,

\begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y} = \frac{3.59 - 4.39}{2.1 - 1.9} = -4, \end{equation*}

(a seemingly very good estimate!) incorporates behavior of \(z\) on both sides of \(y=2\text{.}\)
