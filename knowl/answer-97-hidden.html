1. Venus rotates much more slowly than Earth (roughly \(2.99 \times 10^{-7}\) radians per second). This means the Coriolis effect is far less.
  2. Since the rotation is opposite that of the rotation of Earth, the Coriolis effect is opposite that of the Earth. Winds in the northern hemisphere on Venus would curl as those in the southern hemisphere of earth and vice-versa.
  3. The magnitude of force \(\vec{F}_g\) on a golf ball due to gravity on Venus is \(\vec{F}_g = (45 \ {\rm{g}})(8.87 \ \frac{\rm{m}}{\rm{s}^2}) = (0.045 \ {\rm{kg}})(8.87 \ \frac{\rm{m}}{\rm{s}^2}) = 0.4\) Newtons. The force \(\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\) due to Coriolis has magnitude
    \begin{align*} \|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\| = \mathstrut \amp 2 m v \Omega \sin \varphi \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2 (0.045 \ {\rm{g}})(50 \ {\rm{m/s}})(2.99 \times 10^{-7} \ \frac{1}{\rm{s}}) \sin (45.6^{\circ})\\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 9.645 \times 10^{-7} \ {\rm{Newtons}} \end{align*}
    This is very small comparison to \(\|\vec{F}_g\|\text{.}\) In fact, \(\|\vec{F}_{\rm{Cor}}\|/\|\vec{F}_g\| \approx 2.4 \times 10^{-6} \approx 0.00024\%\text{.}\)
