Example 1.2.17. Mario Kart and Dot Product.

If you have ever played a game called Mario Kart, you are well-positioned to understand the dot product in action. To this end, in Mario Kart, there are ``boost pads" that increase your speed. Imagine the red vector in Figure 1.2.18 is your speed (\(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{j}\) direction), and the blue vector is the orientation of the boost pad (\(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{j}\) direction). Larger numbers are more power. How much boost will you get?
Essentially the pad multiplies your speed.
  • If you come in going 0, you'll get nothing [if you are just dropped onto the pad, there's no boost].
  • If you cross the pad perpendicularly, you'll get 0.
  • If we have some overlap, our \(\vec{i}\)-speed will get an \(\vec{i}\)-boost, and our \(\vec{j}\)-speed gets a \(\vec{j}\)-boost:
    \begin{equation*} Total = speed_{\vec{i}} \cdot boost_{\vec{i}} + speed_{\vec{j}} \cdot boost_{\vec{j}} \end{equation*}
Your incoming speed is \(\|a\|\text{,}\) and the max boost is \(\|b\|\text{.}\) The amount of boost you actually get (for being lined up with it) is \(\cos(\theta)\text{,}\) for the total \(\|a\|\|b\|\cos(\theta)\text{.}\)
