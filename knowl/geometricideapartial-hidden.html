Suppose we are located on the surface \(z=9-x^2-y^2\) at the point \((1,2,4)\text{.}\) As your \(x\) or \(y\) coordinate changes, the value of \(z\) will also change. That is, the output of the function \(f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2\text{,}\) representing your height on the surface, changes as your input, an ordered pair \((x,y)\) changes. If the input is \((1,2)\text{,}\) the output is \(z=4\text{.}\) So how fast does the \(z\)-value, or height on the surface, change if we increase the \(y\)-value while holding the \(x\) value constant (at 1)? That is, as we ``walk'' along the surface in the positive \(y\)-direction?

The surface \(z=9-x^2-y^2\) is shown in FigureÂ 3.1.2 together with the plane \(x=1\text{.}\) We will restrict our movement to this plane. The intersection of these two surfaces is a space curve - the parabola \(z=8-y^2\) parallel to the \(yz-\)plane. The slope of this curve at \((1,2,4)\) is the same as the slope of the parabola \(z=8-y^2\) at \((2,4)\text{.}\) Via calculus, this slope is \(\displaystyle \frac{dz}{dy}=-4\text{.}\) This means that at the point \((1,2,4)\) on the surface, our rate of change in height, \(z\text{,}\) is \(-4\) as we change \(y\text{.}\)
