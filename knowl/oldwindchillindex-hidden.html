Models for the Windchill Index have evolved over time. Until the 1970s, the coldest parts of Canada reported the original Windchill Index, a three or four digit number with units of kilocalories/hour per square meter. Each individual calibrated the scale of numbers personally, through experience. The chart also provided general guidance to comfort and hazard through threshold values of the index, such as 1400, which was the threshold for frostbite. The original formula for the index was
\begin{equation*} W(v,T) = (10\sqrt{v}-v+10.5)(33-T) \end{equation*}
where \(W\) was the Windchill Index, \(v\) was the wind velocity (in \(m/s\)), and \(T\) was the air temperature (in \(^{\circ}C\)). Using this model, \(\frac{\partial W}{\partial T}\) can be found by holding \(v\) constant and differentiating \(W\) with respect to \(T\text{.}\) This gives
\begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{\partial W}{\partial T} = -(10\sqrt{v} - v + 10.5). \end{equation*}
For example, \(\displaystyle W(10,-5) \approx 1220\) (not quite frostbite threshold) and \(\frac{\partial W}{\partial T}(10,-5) = -(10\sqrt{10} - 10 + 10.5) \approx -32.1\text{.}\) This tells us that when the wind velocity is \(10 \ m/s\) and the air temperature is \(-5^{\circ}C\text{,}\) the rate at which the Windchill Index is changing from 1220 kilocalories/hour per square meter is -32.1 (kilocalories/hour per square meter per degree Celsius). Simply put, if wind remained at \(10 \ m/s\) but the air temperature warmed to \(-4^{\circ}C\text{,}\) we could expect the Windchill Index to be roughly 1187.9 (even less worry of frostbite).
