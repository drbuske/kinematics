Example 4.4.31. Using the Flux Formula II.

Suppose that \(\vec{F} = 3\vec{j} + 3\vec{k}\) represents the flow velocity of a fluid, each component in m/sec, and let \(S\) be a 2 meter by 2 meter square screen parallel to the \(xy\)-plane, oriented upward (see Figure 4.4.32). We determine the flux of \(\vec{F}\) through \(S\text{,}\) in cubic meters per second.
The area vector for \(S\) will have magnitude \(2 \times 2 = 4\) and will be pointing upwards (perpendicuar to \(S\)). That is, \(\vec{A} = 4\vec{k}\text{.}\) Using the dot product definition for flux, we see that
\begin{align*} \text{flux} = \mathstrut \amp \vec{F} \cdot \vec{A}\\ = \mathstrut \amp (3\vec{j} + 3\vec{k}) \cdot 4\vec{k}\\ = \mathstrut \amp 12. \end{align*}
Since the units of \(\vec{F}\) are \(\frac{m}{s}\) and the units of \(\vec{A}\) are \(m^2\text{,}\) our result is 12 \(\frac{m^3}{s}\text{.}\) We also note that the angle between the vector field \(\vec{F}\) and the area vector \(\vec{A}\) is \(\theta = 45^{\circ}\text{.}\) It follows that we then also can find the same flux by computing
\begin{gather*} \text{flux} = \|\vec{F}\| \|\vec{A}\| \cos\theta = 3\sqrt{2} \cdot 4 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = 12. \end{gather*}
