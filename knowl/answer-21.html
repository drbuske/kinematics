Answer 1.4.11.1.

  1. Using equations (1.2), with \(x=1\) and \(y=1\) gives \(r^2=1^2+1^2=2\) or \(r=\sqrt{2}\) and \(\tan\theta = \frac{1}{1} = 1\) or \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{4} = 45^{\circ}\text{.}\) The point \((1,1)\) can be represented as either \(\left( \sqrt{2}, 45^{\circ} \right)\) or \(\left( \sqrt{2}, \frac{\pi}{4} \right)\) in polar coordinates.
  2. Again using equations (1.2), but this time with \(x=-3\) and \(y=4\) gives \(r^2=(-3)^2+4^2=25\) or \(r=5\) and \(\tan\theta = -\frac{4}{3}\text{.}\) Since the point \((-3,4)\) lies in quadrant II, we compute \(\theta = \pi+\arctan(-4/3) \approx 2.21\) radians (about 127 degrees).
  3. Here \(r^2=0^2+3^2 = 9\) so that \(r=3\text{.}\) From equations (1.2) we also have \(\tan\theta = \frac{3}{0}\text{.}\) Direct application of the second equation leads to division by zero! Graphing the point \((0,3)\) on the rectangular coordinate system reveals that the point is located on the positive \(y\)-axis. The angle between the positive \(x\)-axis and the positive \(y\)-axis is \(90^{\circ}\) (\(\frac{\pi}{2}\) radians). Therefore this point can be represented as either \(\left( 3, 90^{\circ}\right)\) or \(\left(3,\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\) in polar coordinates.
  4. Using equations (1.2), with \(x=5\sqrt{3}\) and \(y=-5\) gives \(r^2=(5\sqrt{3})^2+(-5)^2=100\) or \(r=10\) and \(\tan\theta = \frac{-5}{5\sqrt{3}} = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\) or \(\theta = -\frac{\pi}{6} = -30^{\circ}\text{.}\) The point \((5\sqrt{3},-5)\) can thus be represented as either \(\left( 10, 330^{\circ}\right)\) or \(\left( 10, -\frac{\pi}{6} \right)\) in polar coordinates.
