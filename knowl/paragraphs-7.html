Vector Resolution.

Suppose a wind is blowing from the NW (generally speaking) and that we are interested in the strength of the westerly component and the (relative) strength of the northerly component. Using components of the wind vector allows us to specify each of these.
In Figure 2.1.32, the displacement vector from \(P\) to \(Q\) is \(3\vec{i}-\vec{j}\text{.}\) Here \(\vec{i}\) is a unit vector pointing east, \(\vec{j}\) is a unit vector pointing north, and \(3\vec{i}\) and \(-\vec{j}\) are the components of the vector.
In a similar fashion, we can resolve any displacement vector \(\vec{v}\) in space by writing it in the form
\begin{gather*} \vec{v} = v_1 \vec{i} + v_2 \vec{j}+v_3 \vec{k}. \end{gather*}
The vectors \(\vec{i}\text{,}\) \(\vec{j}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{k}\) are unit vectors pointing in the direction of the positive \(x\)-axis, the positive \(y\)-axis, and positive \(z\)-axis respectively (see Figure 2.1.33. We call \(v_1 \vec{i}\text{,}\) \(v_2 \vec{j}\text{,}\) and \(v_3 \vec{k}\) the components of \(\vec{v}\text{.}\)

Example 2.1.34. Resolving Geostrophic Wind.

A resolution of the vectors representing both the geostrophic wind \(\vec{v}_g\) and ageostrophic wind \(\vec{v}_{ag}\) given in Example 2.1.24 is \(\vec{v}_g = 2\vec{i} + 3\vec{j}\) and \(\vec{v}_{ag} = 2\vec{i} - \vec{j}\text{.}\) The observed wind \(\vec{v}\) has resolution \(\vec{v}=4\vec{i}+2\vec{j} = (2+2)\vec{i} + (3-1)\vec{j} = (2\vec{i}+3\vec{j}) + (2\vec{i} - \vec{j}) = \vec{v}_g + \vec{v}_{ag}\text{.}\)
