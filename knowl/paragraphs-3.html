Linear Functions in 3-Space.

In 2-space, a linear function is written in the form
\begin{gather*} f(x)=ax+b. \end{gather*}
The graph of \(f\) was a line and the level sets were equally-spaced points on the \(x\)-axis (see Figure 1.3.11). Differentiable functions of one variable have graphs that look like a line when we zoom in. That is, they are locally linear at \(x=x_0\) with slope \(f'(x_0)\text{.}\)
A linear function of two variables is one that can be written in the form
\begin{gather*} f(x)=ax+by+c. \end{gather*}
The graphs of linear functions in two variables are planes and their level sets are equally-spaced parallel lines in the \(xy\)-plane (see Figure 1.3.12). Differentiable functions of two-variables have graphs that look like a plane when we zoom in. They are locally linear at \((x,y)=(x_0,y_0)\) and, just like a plane, have two slopes - one in the \(x\)-direction and one in the \(y\)-direction.
