Example 3.1.9. Algebraic Computation of the Partial Derivative.

Once again we are interested in knowing how fast the dependent variable \(z\) changes with respect to a change in the independent variable \(y\) when \(z = f(x,y) = 9-x^2-y^2\text{.}\) Further, we are interested in this change at the moment that \(x=1, y=2\text{,}\) and \(z=4\text{.}\) We limit our interest to the rate as \(y\) changes; we hold the value of \(x\) constant at \(x=1\text{.}\) We evaluate the difference quotient
\begin{align*} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{f(x,y+\Delta y)-f(x,y)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{(9-x^2-(y+\Delta y)^2) - (9-x^2-y^2)}{\Delta y}. \end{align*}
When we then fix \(x=1\) and let \(\Delta y \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) the partial derivative emerges. That is,
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(1,2) \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta z}{\Delta y}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{f(1,y+\Delta y)-f(1,y)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp \lim_{\Delta y \rightarrow 0}\frac{(8-(y+\Delta y)^2) - (8-y^2)}{\Delta y} \\ = \mathstrut \amp -2y. \end{align*}
When \(y=2\text{,}\) we see that \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(1,2)=-4\text{.}\) Compare this result to those in Checkpoint 3.1.4 and Example 3.1.6. Also, note how closely this calculation resembles that of computing \(g'(2)\) when \(g(y) = 8-y^2\text{.}\)
