1. \(\displaystyle \theta(280,1000) = 280 \left( \frac{1000}{1000} \right)^{0.286} = 280 \ K\)
  2. When \(p < 1000\text{,}\) the base of the exponential part of the function \(\theta(T,p)\) is greater than 1 leading to a potential temperature that is greater than the absolute temperature. That is, \(\theta > T\text{.}\) As \(p\) decreases, the potential temperature \(\theta\) increases.
  3. \(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial T}(T,p) = \left( \frac{1000}{p} \right)^{0.286}\) so that for every one degree increase in absolute temperature \(T\text{,}\) the potential temperature \(\theta\) increases by \(\left( \frac{1000}{p} \right)^{0.286}\text{.}\) Note that this amount of increase does not depend on the initial temperature.
  4. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial p}(T,p) = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial}{\partial p} (1000)^{0.286} p^{-0.286} T \\ = \mathstrut \amp (-0.286)(1000)^{0.286} p^{-1.286} T\\ = \mathstrut \amp -2.06237 \left( \frac{1}{p} \right)^{1.286} T \end{align*}
    Being negative, this means that when holding the absolute temperature constant but increasing the pressure of an air parcel (by, say, having it drop to a lower height in the atmosphere), the potential temperature decreases (and does so at this calculated rate for this temperature \(T\) and pressure \(p\)).
