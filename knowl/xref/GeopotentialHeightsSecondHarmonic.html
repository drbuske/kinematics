Example 2.2.8. Fitting a Trigonometric Polynomial to Geopotential Heights.

If we also use the second harmonic (i.e. take \(m=2\)) on the geopotential height data from ExampleÂ 2.2.5, we then find the best periodic function of the form
\begin{equation*} a_0 + a_1\cos(\omega t) + b_1\sin(\omega t)+a_2\cos(2\omega t) + b_2\sin(2\omega t) \end{equation*}
to fit the data. In addition to the values of \(a_0\text{,}\) \(a_1\text{,}\) and \(b_1\) found earlier, we compute:
\begin{align*} a_2 = \mathstrut \amp \displaystyle \frac{2}{N}\sum y_n \cos(2 \omega t_n) \approx -21.41 \\ b_2 = \mathstrut \amp \displaystyle \frac{2}{N} \sum y_n \sin(2 \omega t_n) \approx 40.21 \end{align*}
The resulting model,
\begin{align*} f(t) = \mathstrut \amp 5656.45 + 44.73\cos\left( \frac{2\pi}{360} t\right) + 12.79\sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{360} t\right) \\ \mathstrut \amp -21.41\cos\left( 2\cdot \frac{2\pi}{360} t\right)+ 40.21\sin\left(2\cdot \frac{2\pi}{360} t\right) \end{align*}
is no longer a sinusoid. However, it remains periodic and does appear to fit the data a bit better.
