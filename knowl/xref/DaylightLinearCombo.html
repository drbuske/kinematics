Example 2.1.16. Modifying the Hours of Daylight Model.

Our model for hours of daylight at 45 degrees North latitude (ExampleÂ 2.1.5) was given by
\begin{equation*} H(t) = 3.4\sin \left( \frac{2\pi}{365}t-\frac{159.5\pi}{365} \right) + 12 \end{equation*}
where \(t\) represents the number of days since the start of the year. Applying trigonometric identity (2.1) gives
\begin{align*} H(t) = \mathstrut \amp 3.4\sin \left( \frac{2\pi}{365}t-\frac{159.5\pi}{365} \right) + 12\\ = \mathstrut \amp 3.4 \left[ \sin \left(\frac{2\pi}{365}t\right)\cos\left(\frac{159.5\pi}{365}\right) - \cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{365}t \right) \sin\left( \frac{159.5\pi}{365}\right) \right] + 12 \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 0.669\sin \left(\frac{2\pi}{365}t\right) - 3.333\cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{365}t \right) + 12. \end{align*}
That is, our sinusoid is now written as a linear combination of \(\sin(\omega t)\text{,}\) \(\cos(\omega t)\text{,}\) and a constant (which can be thought of as \(12\cos(0t)\)).
