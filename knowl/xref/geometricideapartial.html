Example 5.1.2. Geometric Idea of the Partial Derivative.

Suppose we are located on the surface \(z=9-x^2-y^2\) at \((1,2,4)\text{.}\) As your \(x\) or \(y\) coordinate changes, the value of \(z\) will also change. That is, the output of the function \(f(x,y) = 9-x^2 - y^2\text{,}\) representing your height on the surface, changes as your input, an ordered pair \((x,y)\text{,}\) changes. If the input is \((1,2)\text{,}\) the output is \(z=4\text{.}\) So how fast does the \(z\)-value, or height on the surface, change if we increase the \(x\)-value of your location while holding the \(y\) value constant at 2? That is, as we walk along the surface in the positive \(x\)-direction?
The surface \(z=9-x^2-y^2\) is shown in Figure 5.1.3. We will restrict our movement to the plane \(y=2\text{.}\) The intersection of these two surfaces is a space curve - the parabola \(z=5-x^2\) parallel to the \(xz-\)plane. The slope of this curve at \((1,2,4)\) is the same as the slope of the parabola \(z=5-x^2\) at \((1,4)\text{.}\) Using calculus, thd slope of this parabola at \((x_0,z_0)=(1,4)\) is \(\frac{dz}{dx}=-2\text{.}\) This means that at the point \((1,2,4)\) on the surface, our rate of change in height, \(z\text{,}\) is \(-2\) as we change \(x\text{.}\) We denote this fact by writing \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,2) = -2\) and saying that the partial derivative of \(f(x,y)\) with respect to \(x\) at \((1,2)\) is \(-2\text{.}\)
