Example 5.3.7. A Warming Path.

Suppose the temperature (in \(^{\circ}C\)) is given by the function \(T(x,y)\) whose contours are shown in Figure 5.3.8 (\(x, y\) are in kilometers). If you consider how your location corresponds to points in the \(xy\)-plane, you know that when you pass the point \((2,1)\text{,}\) your velocity vector is \(-\vec{i} + 2\vec{j}\) (km/hr). We estimate the rate of change of your temperature, \(\frac{dT}{dt}\text{,}\) when passing through \((2,1)\text{.}\)
First, note that the dependence diagram from Example 5.3.5 with \(T\) replacing variable \(P\) applies here.
We have
\begin{equation*} \frac{dT}{dt} = \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{x}} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{y}} \frac{dy}{dt}. \end{equation*}
Next, note that when \(x = 2\) and \(y=-1\text{,}\) the velocity vector tells us that \(\frac{dx}{dt} = -1\) and \(\frac{dy}{dt} = 2\text{.}\) Using the contours, we can estimate \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \approx \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta x}\) and \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial y} \approx \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta y}\) to obtain
\begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{x}} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial{T}}{\partial{y}} \frac{dy}{dt} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp \left( \frac{2-4}{3-1.5} \right)\frac{^{\circ}C}{km} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + \left( \frac{4-2}{1.75-0.25} \right)\frac{^{\circ}C}{km} \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp (-1.33 ^{\circ}C/km)\left(-1 \ \frac{km}{hr}\right) + (1.33 ^{\circ}C/km)\left(2 \ \frac{km}{hr}\right) \\ \approx \mathstrut \amp 4 \ \frac{^{\circ}C}{hr}. \end{align*}
This tells us that as we move in the direction of velocity vector at \((2,1)\text{,}\) the temperature will rise (which is clear from the contour map). But it also tells us how quickly the temperature will rise. Moreover, you can see the contributions to this rate of change that come from our movement in the horizontal direction (1.33 \(^{\circ}C/hr\)) and our movement in the vertical direction (2.66 \(^{\circ}C/hr\)). We warm as we move west, but we warm even faster, roughly twice as fast, as we move north.
