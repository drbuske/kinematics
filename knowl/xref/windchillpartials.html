Checkpoint 5.1.20. Interpreting the Meaning of a Wind Chill Partial.

Let \(C = f(w,T)\) where \(C\) is the wind chill temperature (in degrees Fahrenheit) that is associated with a wind speed of \(w\) miles per hour and a temperature of \(T\) degrees Fahrenheit. A table of values for the function \(f\) is given in TableÂ 5.1.21.
  1. Estimate \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(10,25)\text{.}\) What does your answer mean in practical terms?
  2. Estimate \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial T}(5,20)\text{.}\) What does your answer mean in practical terms?
  3. When the temperature is \(20^{\circ}F\text{,}\) the temperature adjusted for wind chill drops by an average of about \(0.8^{\circ}F\) with every 1 mph increase in wind speed from 5 mph to 10 mph. Which partial derivative is this telling you about?
Answer.
  1. \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(10,25) \approx \frac{13-19}{15-5} = -0.6 \frac{^{\circ}F}{\rm{mph}}\text{;}\) in practical terms, this means that when the wind is blowing at 10 mph and the temperature is \(25^{\circ}F\text{,}\) then it will feel roughly -0.6 degrees colder for every 1 mph increase in wind speed. That is, this is the rate as which the windchill changes with respect to a change in wind speed.
  2. \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial T}(5,20) \approx \frac{19-7}{25-15} = 1.2\frac{^{\circ}F}{^{\circ}F} = 1.2\text{;}\) in practical terms, when the wind is blowing at 5 mph and the temperature is \(20^{\circ}F\text{,}\) then it will feel roughly 1.2 degrees Fahrenheit warmer for every 1 degree Fahrenheit the temperature increases - again a rate of change.
  3. This suggests that \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(5,20)\) and \(\frac{\partial C}{\partial w}(10,20)\) are both approximately \(-0.8 \frac{^{\circ}F}{\rm{mph}}\text{.}\)
