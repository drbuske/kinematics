Example 4.6.17.

The graph of \(u(x,y)=e^{-\frac{1}{3}x^3+x-y^2}\) appears in FigureÂ 4.6.18. We have
\begin{equation*} \nabla u = (-x^2+1)e^{-\frac{1}{3}x^3+x-y^2} \vec{i} -2ye^{-\frac{1}{3}x^3+x-y^2}\vec{j}. \end{equation*}
From this,
\begin{align*} \Delta u = \mathstrut \amp \nabla \cdot (\nabla u)\\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}\\ = \mathstrut \amp (-2x+(1-x^2)^2-2+4y^2)e^{-\frac{1}{3}x^3+x-y^2} \end{align*}
At \((1,0.5)\text{,}\) we then find \(\Delta u(1,0.5) \lt 0\text{.}\) From this we conclude that the average value of \(u\) near \((1,0.5)\) is less than the value of \(u\) at \((1,0.5)\text{.}\) Note that the gradient vector field \(\nabla u\) has a sink nearby at \((1,0)\text{.}\) So it makes sense that the divergence of this gradient vector field would be negative at \((1,0.5)\text{.}\)
