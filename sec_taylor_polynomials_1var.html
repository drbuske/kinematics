    Section 7.1 Taylor Polynomials in One Variable

    Motivating Questions

    • Tangent lines are used in calculus to approximate the graph of a function near a point. Could a parabola do a better job of approximation near a point? If so, how do we find such a parabola?
    • Could a higher degree polynomial be used to approximate the graph of a function near a given point?
    Meteorological phenomena can be complicated. Earlier we modeled geopotential heights at a given latitude using trigonometric polynomials. We used sine and cosine functions because the data was periodic. But what about modeling behavior or functions that are not periodic? And what about modeling functions of more than one variable? In this section, we use polynomials called Taylor polynomials to model such functions.

    Subsection 7.1.1 Taylor Polynomials of Degree 2

    Example 7.1.1. Polynomials approximating \(f(x)=e^{-x}\).

    For illustration purposes, we start by finding a polynomial that fits a rather simple function, \(f(x)=e^{-x}\text{.}\) And, we will try to fit the polynomial well near \(x=0\text{.}\) If the polynomial has degree 0, call it \(P_0(x)\text{,}\) then it will simply be a constant function. What is the â€™bestâ€™ constant function to approximate \(f(x)=e^{-x}\) near \(x=0\text{?}\) \(P_0(x)=1\) will do since it is an exact fit at \(x=0\text{.}\) Sure, it isnâ€™t a very good fit far away from \(x=0\text{,}\) but we seek polynomial fits that are good near \(x=0\) and so \(P_0(x)=1\) is as good as any constant function can approximate \(f(x)\text{.}\) We will call \(P_0(x)\) the zero-degree Taylor polynomial approximation to \(f(x)=e^{-x}\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    Next, letâ€™s find a polynomial of degree 1, call it \(P_1(x)\text{,}\) that fits \(f(x)=e^{-x}\) well near \(x=0\text{.}\) Since a polynomial of degree one is just a linear function, the polynomial whose graph is the tangent line to \(f(x)\) at \(x=0\) would work well since we would have that \(f(0)=P_1(0)\) and \(f'(0)=P_1(0)\text{.}\) That is, both the value of \(P_1(x)\) and the derivative or slope of \(P_1(x)\) would match the function perfectly at \(x=0\text{.}\) From calculus, you may remember that this tangent line is given by the function \(P_1(x) = f(0) +f'(0)(x-0) = 1 + (-1)x = 1-x\) when \(f(x)=e^{-x}\text{.}\) We call \(P_1(x)\) (the linearization of \(f(x)=e^{-x}\) at \(x=0\)) the Taylor polynomial approximation of degree 1.
    To determine a polynomial of degree 2, call it \(P_2(x)\) that fits \(f(x)=e^{-x}\) well near 0, we should think about what we mean by the phrase â€™fits well.â€™ No matter how hard we try, a polynomial of degree 2 (whose graph is always a parabola) will never fit \(f(x)=e^{-x}\) perfectly everywhere. So letâ€™s focus on getting good attributes at and near \(x=0\text{.}\) For example, with the Taylor polynomial \(P_1(x)\text{,}\) we saw that \(P_1(0)=f(0)\) and \(P_1'(0)=f'(0)\text{.}\) That is the height and slope of \(f(x)\) at \(x=0\) matched \(P_1(x)\text{.}\) It seems reasonable to then also ask \(P_2''(0)=f''(0)\text{.}\) That is, to ask the concavity of both functions, \(f(x)\) and \(P_2(x)\text{,}\) to match at \(x=0\text{.}\) If we define
    \begin{equation*} P_2(x)=c_0 + c_1x + c_2x^2 \end{equation*}
    and if we require that
    \begin{equation*} P_2(0)=f(0), \ \ \ P_2'(0)=f'(0), \ \ \ \ {\rm{and}} \ \ \ \ P_2''(0)=f''(0) \end{equation*}
    then it turns out that
    \begin{align*} \displaystyle c_0 = \mathstrut \amp f(0)=1 \\ c_1 = \mathstrut \amp f'(0)=-1 \\ c_2 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{f''(0)}{2} = \frac{1}{2}. \end{align*}
    Graphs of Taylor polynomial approximations of degrees 0, 1, and 2 appear in FigureÂ 7.1.2.
    Taylor Polynomials of Degree 1 and 2.
    Given a smooth function \(f(x)\text{,}\) the Taylor polynomial of degree one about \(x=x_0\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} P_1(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0). \end{equation*}
    This is the same as the tangent line to \(f(x)\) at \(x=x_0\text{.}\) The Taylor polynomial of degree two about \(x=x_0\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} P_2(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{f''(x_0)}{2}(x-x_0). \end{equation*}

    Example 7.1.3. Approximating Potential Temperature.

    The potential temperature \(\theta\) of a parcel of air at pressure \(p\) (in mb) is the temperature that the air parcel would attain if adiabatically brought to a standard reference pressure such as \(p_0=1000\) mb. Assuming the air is an ideal gas, if \(T\) is the current absolute temperature of the air parcel (in Kelvin), then
    \begin{equation*} \theta(T,p) = T \left( \frac{1000}{p} \right)^{0.286}. \end{equation*}
    If the temperature of the air parcel is held constant at \(T=10^{\circ}C\) (283 Kelvin), then the potential temperature (in Kelvin) would be given by the one-variable function
    \begin{equation*} \theta(p) = 283\left( \frac{1000}{p}\right)^{0.286}. \end{equation*}
    The Taylor polynomial approximation of degree 2 at \(p=500\) mb is then given by
    \begin{equation*} P_2(p) = c_0 + c_1(p-500) + c_2(p-500)^2 \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{align*} \displaystyle c_0 = \mathstrut \amp \theta(500) = 283\left( \frac{1000}{500} \right)^{0.286} \approx 345.05 \\ c_1 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\theta'(500)}{1!} \approx -0.197 \\ c_2 = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\theta''(500)}{2!} \approx 0.000254. \end{align*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \theta(p) \approx P_2(p) = 345.05 -0.197(p-500) + 0.000254(p-500)^2. \end{equation*}
    Both \(\theta(p)\) and \(P_2(p)\) are plotted in FigureÂ 7.1.4. Note the good fit near \(p=500\) mb and poor fit at \(p=100\) mb.

    Subsection 7.1.2 Taylor Polynomials of Degree \(n\)

    Taylor Polynomials of Degree \(n\).

    In general, the Taylor polynomial of degree \(n\) approximating any function \(f(x)\) near \(x=0\) has the form
    \begin{equation*} P_n(x) = c_0 + c_1x+ c_2x^2 + c_3x^3 + \dots + c_nx^n \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} c_0=f(0), c_1 = \frac{f'(0)}{1!}, c_2 = \frac{f''(0)}{2!}, c_3 = \frac{f'''(0)}{3!}, \dots, c_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!}. \end{equation*}
    If we wish to approximate using a Taylor polynomial near a different point, say \(x=a\text{,}\) then it has the form
    \begin{equation*} P_n(x) = c_0 + c_1(x-a)+ c_2(x-a)^2 + c_3(x-a)^3 + \dots + c_n(x-a)^n \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} c_0=f(a), c_1 = \frac{f'(a)}{1!}, c_2 = \frac{f''(a)}{2!}, c_3 = \frac{f'''(a)}{3!}, \dots, c_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}. \end{equation*}
