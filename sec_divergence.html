    Section 6.5 Divergence

    Motivating Questions

    • How can we understand the concept of divergence both geometrically and algebraically?
    • Why might we only be concerned with the horizontal divergence of the wind? How would we compute this?
    In this section, we use the flux out of a closed surface surrounding a point to measure the outflow per unit volume there, also called the divergence, or flux density. To measure the outflow per unit volume of a vector field at a point, we calculate the flux out of a small sphere (or cube) centered at the point, divide by the volume enclosed by the sphere (or cube), then take the limit of this flux-to-volume ratio as the sphere (or cube) contracts around the point.

    Definition 6.5.2. Geometric Definition of Divergence.

    The divergence, or flux density, of a smooth vector field \(\vec{F}\) is a scalar-valued function given by
    \begin{gather*} div \ \vec{F} (x,y,z) = \lim_{Volume \rightarrow 0} \frac{\int_S \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{A}}{\text{Volume of }S} \end{gather*}
    where \(S\) is a sphere centered at \((x,y,z)\text{,}\) oriented outward, that contracts to the point \((x,y,z)\) in the limit.
    We note that \(div \ \vec{F}(x,y,z)\) is negative when there is net flow into the point \((x,y,z)\) and positive when there is net flow out of the point \((x,y,z)\text{.}\)

    Example 6.5.3. Sign of Divergence.

    For the vector field \(\vec{F}\) illustrated in FigureÂ 6.5.4, the divergence at the origin is negative since there is a net flow into that point. All of the flow appears to be towards the origin as it passes through the surface of the sphere. If the surface of this sphere \(S\) were oriented outward, then the flux of \(\vec{F}\) across \(S\) would be negative. Hence the flux density (i.e. the divergence) at the origin is also negative.
    For the vector field \(\vec{F}\) illustrated in FigureÂ 6.5.5, the divergence at the origin is positive since there is a net flow out of that point. While a small amount of the flow appears to be moving towards the origin from the left, much more flow is moving away towards the right. If the surface of this sphere \(S\) were oriented outward, then the flux of \(\vec{F}\) across \(S\) (no matter the size of \(S\text{!}\)) would be positive. The divergence, or flux density, at the origin is then also positive.

    Checkpoint 6.5.6. Sign of Divergence II.

    For the constant vector field \(\vec{F}\) shown in FigureÂ 6.5.7, determine if the divergence at the origin is positive, negative, or zero.
    Answer.
    Since the same amount of flow goes into the sphere as goes out of the sphere, the divergence (net flow) at the origin is zero. The flux of the vector field \(\vec{F}\) across this sphere is zero. The flux density will then also be zero.
    In atmospheric kinematics, the term convergence refers to the accumulation of air in a region or layer of the atmosphere. This is, mathematically speaking, negative divergence. A vector field \(\vec{v}\) representing wind can converge or diverge due to either direction or speed as shown in FigureÂ 6.5.8 and FigureÂ 6.5.9.
    While the geometric definition is useful for understanding the concept of divergence, the ability to calculate is often better supported by an algebraic definition. To that end, we now present an equivalent algebraic definition of divergence of a vector field \(\vec{F}\text{.}\)

    Definition 6.5.10. Algebraic (Coordinate) Definition of Divergence.

    If \(\vec{F} = F_1\vec{i} + F_2\vec{j} + F_3\vec{k}\text{,}\) we can calculate the divergence of \(\vec{F}\) by
    \begin{gather*} div \ \vec{F} = \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}. \end{gather*}
    A few remarks regarding notation we will use for divergence. If we write the â€™delâ€™ operator as
    \begin{gather*} \nabla = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \vec{i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \vec{j} + \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \vec{k} \end{gather*}
    and
    \begin{gather*} \vec{F} = F_1\vec{i} + F_2\vec{j} + F_3\vec{k} \end{gather*}
    then we have
    \begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \vec{F} = \mathstrut \amp \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \vec{i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \vec{j} + \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \vec{k}\right) \cdot \left( F_1\vec{i} + F_2\vec{j} + F_3\vec{k}\right)\\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}\\ = \mathstrut \amp div \ \vec{F}. \end{align*}

    Example 6.5.11. A Sink.

    The vector field \(\vec{F}\) illustrated in FigureÂ 6.5.4 has algebraic description of \(\vec{F}(x,y,z) = -x\vec{i}-y\vec{j}-z\vec{k}\text{.}\) From this formula, we compute the divergence:
    \begin{align*} div \ \vec{F} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial }{\partial x}(-x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-y) + \frac{\partial }{\partial z}(-z)\\ = \mathstrut \amp -1 -1 -1 \\ = \mathstrut \amp -3. \end{align*}
    at every point \((x,y,z)\) and hence also at \((0,0,0)\text{.}\) Note that we also found negative divergence in ExampleÂ 6.5.3, but this computation quantifies this value. When a point located in a vector field \(\vec{F}\) such as \((0,0,0)\) has negative divergence, we call it a sink. Conversely, when a point \(P\) has positive divergence (i.e. \(div \ \vec{F}(P) > 0\)) in a vector field \(\vec{F}\text{,}\) we call it a source.

    Checkpoint 6.5.12. Sink, Source, or Neither?

    The vector field \(\vec{F}\) illustrated in FigureÂ 6.5.5 has algebraic description of \(\vec{F}(x,y,z) = x\vec{i} +(2y+1)\vec{j} + 2z\vec{k}\text{.}\) Determine \(div \ \vec{F}(0,0,0)\) and decide if the origin is a source, a sink, or neither.
    Answer.
    We compute
    \begin{align*} div \ \vec{F} = \mathstrut \amp \frac{\partial }{\partial x}(x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2y+1) + \frac{\partial }{\partial z}(2z)\\ = \mathstrut \amp 1+2+2 \\ = \mathstrut \amp 5. \end{align*}
    Since \(div \ \vec{F}(0,0,0) = 5 > 0\text{,}\) we conclude that the origin is a source. In fact, every point \((x,y,z)\) in this vector field \(\vec{F}\) is a source.

    Example 6.5.13. Divergence on the San Francisco Coast.

    At a distance of \(1000\) km off the coast of San Francisco, the wind \(\vec{v} = u\vec{i} + v\vec{j} + w\vec{k}\) blows due east.
    • At \(400 \ mb\text{,}\) the wind is \(\vec{v} = (60 \ km/hr)\vec{i}\text{.}\)
    • At \(300 \ mb\text{,}\) the wind is \(\vec{v} = (100 \ km/hr)\vec{i}\text{.}\)
    • At \(200 \ mb\text{,}\) the wind is \(\vec{v} = (180 \ km/hr)\vec{i}\text{.}\)
    In the city, the wind is calm (\(\vec{v} = \vec{0}\)).
    We estimate the divergence \(\nabla \cdot \vec{v}\) on the San Francisco coast at each level of the atmosphere.
    • At \(400 \ mb\text{,}\) \(\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} + 0 + 0 = \frac{0-60 \ km/hr}{1000 \ km} = -0.06 \frac{1}{hr}\text{.}\)
    • At \(300 \ mb\text{,}\) \(\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} = \frac{0-100 \ km/hr}{1000 \ km} = -0.1 \frac{1}{hr}\text{.}\)
    • At \(200 \ mb\text{,}\) \(\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} = \frac{0-180 \ km/hr}{1000 \ km} = -0.18 \frac{1}{hr}\text{.}\)
    At each level of the atmosphere, the divergence is negative. That is, we have convergence.

    Definition 6.5.15. Horizontal Divergence.

    Horizontal divergence is a measure of the local spreading of a vector field \(\vec{v}\) (such as wind) in a horizontal plane. We commonly denote this by \(\nabla_H \cdot \vec{v}\text{.}\)
    If \(u\) and \(v\) are the components of the vector field \(\vec{v}\) along the horizontal axes \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) then
    \begin{gather*} \nabla_H \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}. \end{gather*}

    Checkpoint 6.5.16. A Simple Horizontal Wind.

    The vector field \(\vec{v}\) in FigureÂ 6.5.17 represents the horizontal wind (positive \(x\) is to the right, positive \(y\) is up as usual). Is the horizontal divergence at the point positive, negative, or zero?
    Answer.
    \(\displaystyle \nabla_H \cdot \vec{v} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \approx \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} + \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta y}\text{.}\) Since the east-west component of the wind (i.e. \(u\)) is not changing as we move to the east, we have \(\displaystyle \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x}=0\text{.}\) Similarly, since the north-south component of the wind (i.e. \(v=0\)) is not changing as we move to the north, we also have \(\displaystyle \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta y}=0\text{.}\) It follows that the horizontal divergence \(\nabla_H \cdot \vec{v} = 0\text{.}\)

    Example 6.5.18. Calculating Horizontal Divergence.

    The following wind data were received from \(50\) km to the east, north, west and south of a station \(A\text{,}\) respectively: \(90^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(10\) m/s; \(120^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(4\) m/s; \(90^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(8\) m/s; \(60^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(4\) m/s. We calculate the approximate horizontal divergence at the station.
    The horizontal divergence can be approximated by
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \approx \mathstrut \amp \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} + \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta y}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{[-10 - (-8)]\ m/s}{100\ km} + \frac{[2-(-2)]\ m/s}{100\ km}\\ = \mathstrut \amp \frac{2 \ m/s}{100,000\ m} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 2 \times 10^{-5} \frac{1}{s}. \end{align*}
    Note the units of divergence are 1/s.

    Checkpoint 6.5.20. Calculating Horizontal Divergence.

    Suppose the following wind data were received from \(100\) km to the east, north, west and south of a station \(B\text{,}\) respectively: \(270^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(8\) m/s; \(300^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(6\) m/s; \(270^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(6\)6 m/s; \(300^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(2\) m/s. Calculate the approximate horizontal divergence at the station.
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} \displaystyle \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \approx \mathstrut \amp \frac{(8-6) \ m/s}{200 \ km} + \frac{(-6\cos(60^{\circ})-(-2\cos(60^{\circ}))) \ m/s}{200 \ km} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 0.00001 \frac{1}{s} - 0.00001 \frac{1}{s} \\ = \mathstrut \amp 0 \frac{1}{s} \end{align*}
    To a good approximation, density of air does not change with time for any given horizontal surface. Density decreases with height, but for each height level, density is fairly constant. So, for wind \(\vec{v} = u\vec{i} + v\vec{j} + w\vec{k}\text{,}\) we have (due to mass conservation)
    \begin{gather*} \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = 0. \end{gather*}
    Separating out the horizontal and vertical components gives
    \begin{gather*} \nabla \cdot \vec{v} = \nabla_H \cdot \vec{v}_H + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}=0. \end{gather*}
    From this, \(\nabla_H \cdot \vec{v}_H > 0\) means \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial z} < 0\) and \(\nabla_H \cdot \vec{v}_H < 0 \) means \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial z} > 0\text{.}\) We conclude
    • Horizontal divergence results in the horizontal stretching of an air mass, potentially increasing its stability.
    • Horizontal convergence causes a mass of air to rise (increase its upward vertical velocity), which could decrease the stability of the air mass.
    Just like the ground is a lower boundary on movement of air mass, the tropopause is an upper boundary. Vertical motion caused by convergence or divergence impacts air pressure and thus weather (see FigureÂ 6.5.22).
    • Horizontal convergence in the upper troposphere increases downward vertical velocity which increases surface pressure (high pressure, clear sky).
    • Horizontal divergence in the upper troposphere increases upward vertical velocity which decreases surface pressure (low pressure, cloudy).
